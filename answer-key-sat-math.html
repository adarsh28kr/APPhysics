Algebra (Q1–Q7)
Q1 — Linear Equations Easy Answer: (C) $17$
Step 1: Distribute: $3(x - 4) = 3x - 12$
Step 2: Equation becomes $3x - 12 = 2x + 5$
Step 3: Subtract $2x$: $x - 12 = 5$
Step 4: Add $12$: $x = 17$
Strategy: Always distribute first, then isolate. Move variables to one side, constants to the other. Check by plugging back in: $3(17-4) = 3(13) = 39$ and $2(17)+5 = 39$. ✓
Common mistakes: (A) $7$ — forgot to distribute the $3$ to the $-4$, writing $3x - 4 = 2x + 5$. (D) $-1$ — sign error when moving $12$ across.
Q2 — Linear Inequalities Easy Answer: (A) $0$
Step 1: $-2x + 7 > 3$
Step 2: Subtract $7$: $-2x > -4$
Step 3: Divide by $-2$ (FLIP the sign): $x < 2$
Step 4: Only $x = 0$ from the choices satisfies $x < 2$.
Strategy: Dividing or multiplying by a negative FLIPS the inequality. This is tested on almost every SAT. Memorize the rule.
Gotcha: If you forget to flip: $x > 2$, you'd pick (C) $3$ or (D) $5$ — both wrong.
Common mistakes: (B) $2$ — boundary value, but $x < 2$ is strict (not $\le$). (C)/(D) — forgot to flip the inequality sign.
Q3 — Systems (Substitution) Medium Answer: (B) $8$
Step 1: Substitute $y = 2x - 1$ into $3x + y = 14$:
Step 2: $3x + (2x - 1) = 14 \Rightarrow 5x - 1 = 14$
Step 3: $5x = 15 \Rightarrow x = 3$
Step 4: $y = 2(3) - 1 = 5$
Step 5: $x + y = 3 + 5 = 8$
Strategy: When one equation is already solved for a variable (like $y = ...$), substitution is faster than elimination.
Common mistakes: (A) $5$ — gave only $y$, not $x + y$. (D) $14$ — the RHS of the second equation, not the answer. Always read what the question asks!
Q4 — Linear Word Problem Medium Answer: (B) $5$
Step 1: Translate: Total $=$ Service fee $+$ Hourly rate $\times$ Hours
Step 2: $225 = 50 + 35h$
Step 3: $35h = 175$
Step 4: $h = 5$
Strategy: Word problems → equation. Identify: fixed cost ($50), rate ($35/hr), and total ($225). The structure is always: Total = Fixed + Rate × Variable.
Common mistakes: (C) $6$ — dividing $225 \div 35 \approx 6.4$ without subtracting the $50 fee first. (A) $4$ — arithmetic error $175/35$.
Q5 — Systems (Elimination) Medium Answer: (B) $2$
Step 1: Notice $+3y$ and $-3y$ → add equations directly.
Step 2: $(2x + 3y) + (4x - 3y) = 12 + 6$
Step 3: $6x = 18 \Rightarrow x = 3$
Step 4: Substitute into first: $2(3) + 3y = 12 \Rightarrow 3y = 6 \Rightarrow y = 2$
Strategy: Look for coefficients that cancel when adding or subtracting. Here $+3y$ and $-3y$ cancel immediately — no multiplication needed.
Gotcha: The question asks for $y$, not $x$. Finding $x = 3$ is only halfway done.
Common mistakes: (C) $3$ — gave $x$ instead of $y$. This is the #1 careless error on systems problems.
Q6 — Absolute Value Hard Answer: (B) $4$
Step 1: Split into two cases:
Case 1: $3x - 6 = 12 \Rightarrow 3x = 18 \Rightarrow x = 6$
Case 2: $3x - 6 = -12 \Rightarrow 3x = -6 \Rightarrow x = -2$
Step 2: Sum of all solutions: $6 + (-2) = 4$
Strategy: $|A| = B$ splits into $A = B$ or $A = -B$ (provided $B \ge 0$). Always find ALL solutions, then answer the actual question (sum, product, etc.).
Gotcha: The question asks for the SUM, not individual solutions. (C) $6$ is only one solution.
Common mistakes: (C) $6$ — found only the positive solution and stopped. (A) $2$ — computed $6 - (-2) = 8$... no wait, that gives $8$. Actually (A) could come from $6 + (-2)/2 = 2$ (accidentally averaging).
Q7 — Infinite Solutions Hard Answer: (C) $8$
Step 1: Expand left side: $4(2x + 3) = 8x + 12$
Step 2: Equation becomes: $8x + 12 = kx + 12$
Step 3: For infinitely many solutions, both sides must be identical.
Step 4: Coefficients of $x$ must match: $k = 8$. Constants already match ($12 = 12$). ✓
Strategy: Infinitely many solutions = identical expressions (all $x$ work). No solutions = contradiction ($0 = \text{nonzero}$). One solution = normal case. This is a high-value concept on the SAT.
Gotcha: (D) $12$ — matching the constant, but the constants already match regardless of $k$. The constraint comes from the $x$-coefficients.
Common mistakes: (B) $4$ — dividing $8$ by $2$ for no reason. (A) $3$ — confusing the constant in the parentheses with the answer.
Advanced Math (Q8–Q14)
Q8 — Exponent Rules Easy Answer: (B) $27$
Step 1: Numerator: $3^5 \cdot 3^2 = 3^{5+2} = 3^7$
Step 2: Divide: $3^7 / 3^4 = 3^{7-4} = 3^3$
Step 3: $3^3 = 27$
Strategy: Three rules: $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$, $a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}$, $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$. Don't compute large powers — simplify the exponent first.
Common mistakes: (A) $9 = 3^2$ — subtracted exponents wrong ($7-4 \ne 2$). (C) $81 = 3^4$ — forgot to add in the numerator.
Q9 — Factoring Quadratics Easy Answer: (C) $x = 2$ and $x = 3$
Step 1: Need two numbers that multiply to $+6$ and add to $-5$.
Step 2: $(-2)(-3) = 6$ ✓ and $(-2) + (-3) = -5$ ✓
Step 3: Factor: $(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$
Step 4: Solutions: $x = 2$ or $x = 3$
Strategy: For $x^2 + bx + c$: find two numbers with product $= c$ and sum $= b$. Signs matter!
Gotcha: The factored form is $(x-2)(x-3)$, so solutions are $+2$ and $+3$. Don't confuse the signs in the factors with the signs of the solutions.
Common mistakes: (B) $x = -2, -3$ — correct magnitudes but wrong signs. The factors have minus signs, but that means the roots are positive.
Q10 — Discriminant Medium Answer: (B) $9$
Step 1: One real solution $\Rightarrow$ discriminant $= 0$
Step 2: $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = 6^2 - 4(1)(c) = 36 - 4c$
Step 3: Set $\Delta = 0$: $36 - 4c = 0 \Rightarrow c = 9$
Strategy: Discriminant determines the number of solutions. For exactly one solution (a "double root"), set $b^2 - 4ac = 0$ and solve for the unknown.
Common mistakes: (D) $36$ — this is $b^2$, not $c$. You need $c = b^2/(4a) = 36/4 = 9$. (A) $6$ — confused $b$ with $c$.
Q11 — Factor Theorem Medium Answer: (B) $(x - 2)$
Step 1: Given $p(2) = 0$, by the Factor Theorem, $(x - 2)$ is a factor.
Verify: $p(2) = 8 - 16 + 2 + 6 = 0$ ✓
Strategy: Factor Theorem: $p(a) = 0 \Leftrightarrow (x - a)$ is a factor. The sign flips! $p(2) = 0$ gives $(x - 2)$, not $(x + 2)$.
Gotcha: (A) $(x+2)$ is the most common wrong answer. Remember: zero at $x = 2$ means factor $(x - 2)$.
Q12 — Exponential Growth Medium Answer: (B) $500 \cdot 2^{t/3}$
Step 1: Initial amount: $500$. Growth factor: $2$ (doubling). Period: $3$ hours.
Step 2: After $t$ hours, number of doubling periods $= t/3$.
Step 3: Formula: $N = 500 \cdot 2^{t/3}$
Check: At $t = 3$: $500 \cdot 2^1 = 1000$ (doubled). At $t = 6$: $500 \cdot 2^2 = 2000$ (doubled again). ✓
Strategy: Exponential model: $N = N_0 \cdot b^{t/T}$. The exponent is always (time elapsed) ÷ (period for one cycle). Doubling $\Rightarrow b = 2$.
Common mistakes: (A) $2^{3t}$ — this puts the period in the wrong spot; it should divide $t$, not multiply. (D) linear growth, not exponential.
Q13 — Rational Equations Hard Answer: (A) $-5$
Step 1: Multiply everything by $(x-2)(x+2)$:
Step 2: $x(x+2) + 1(x-2) = 8$
Step 3: $x^2 + 2x + x - 2 = 8 \Rightarrow x^2 + 3x - 10 = 0$
Step 4: Factor: $(x + 5)(x - 2) = 0 \Rightarrow x = -5$ or $x = 2$
Step 5: Check: $x = 2$ makes $(x-2) = 0$ in the denominator. REJECT.
Step 6: Only valid solution: $x = -5$
Strategy: After solving a rational equation, ALWAYS plug solutions back into the original denominators. Any value that makes a denominator zero is extraneous and must be rejected.
Gotcha: $x = 2$ is a trap. It satisfies the quadratic but NOT the original equation. This is exactly what the SAT tests with rational equations.
Common mistakes: (B) $2$ — a valid algebraic solution but extraneous (makes denominator $0$). (C) "$-5$ and $2$" — didn't check for extraneous solutions.
Q14 — Function Composition Hard Answer: (B) $3$
Step 1: Build the composite: $f(g(x)) = 2(x^2 - 3) + 1 = 2x^2 - 5$
Step 2: Set equal to $13$: $2x^2 - 5 = 13$
Step 3: $2x^2 = 18 \Rightarrow x^2 = 9 \Rightarrow x = \pm 3$
Step 4: Positive value: $x = 3$
Strategy: For $f(g(x))$, work inside-out: first write $g(x)$, then plug it into $f$. Simplify the result, then solve the equation.
Gotcha: Don't confuse $f(g(x))$ with $g(f(x))$. Order matters! Also, $x^2 = 9$ gives $\pm 3$, but the question specifies "positive."
Common mistakes: (D) $9$ — this is $x^2$, not $x$. Always take the square root as the final step. (A) $2$ — computed $g(x)$ wrong.
Problem Solving & Data Analysis (Q15–Q17)
Q15 — Ratios Easy Answer: (C) $18$
Step 1: Ratio $2:3$ means total parts $= 2 + 3 = 5$
Step 2: Each part $= 30 \div 5 = 6$ students
Step 3: Girls $= 3 \times 6 = 18$
Strategy: In a ratio $a:b$, the second group is $\frac{b}{a+b}$ of the total. Here: $\frac{3}{5} \times 30 = 18$.
Common mistakes: (A) $12$ — this is the number of boys ($2 \times 6$), not girls. (B) $15$ — assumed equal split.
Q16 — Line of Best Fit Medium Answer: (C) $68$
Step 1: Substitute $x = 8$ into $y = -1.5x + 80$
Step 2: $y = -1.5(8) + 80 = -12 + 80 = 68$
Strategy: A line of best fit is just a linear equation. Plug in the given $x$-value and compute. The slope tells you the rate of change: here, each additional hour of exercise predicts $1.5$ bpm lower heart rate.
Gotcha: The slope is negative. If you miss the negative sign: $1.5(8) + 80 = 92$, which isn't even a choice — a clue you made a sign error.
Q17 — Mean = Median Hard Answer: (D) $11$
Step 1: With 7 values, the median is the 4th value when sorted.
Step 2: If $x \ge 10$ (as we'll verify), sorted order is: $3, 5, 7, 8, 10, x, 12$ or $3, 5, 7, 8, 10, 11, 12$. Either way, the median is $8$.
Step 3: Set mean $= 8$: $\frac{3 + 5 + 7 + 8 + 10 + 12 + x}{7} = 8$
Step 4: $45 + x = 56 \Rightarrow x = 11$
Verify: Sorted: $3, 5, 7, 8, 10, 11, 12$. Median $= 8$. Mean $= 56/7 = 8$. ✓
Strategy: When mean = median, figure out what the median would be (it depends on where $x$ falls in the sorted order). Then set the mean equal to that value and solve for $x$. Check that $x$'s position doesn't change the median.
Gotcha: You must verify that $x = 11$ doesn't change the position of the median. Since $11$ slots between $10$ and $12$, the 4th value is still $8$. ✓
Common mistakes: (A) $8$ — assumed $x$ must equal the median. But $x = 8$ gives mean $= 53/7 \approx 7.6 \ne 8$.
Geometry & Trigonometry (Q18–Q20)
Q18 — Triangle Angles Easy Answer: (C) $65°$
Step 1: Angles in a triangle sum to $180°$.
Step 2: $\angle C = 180° - 50° - 65° = 65°$
Strategy: This is the most fundamental geometry fact: triangle angles sum to $180°$. Quick mental math.
Common mistakes: (A) $55°$ — subtracted only one angle: $180 - 50 - 75$... arithmetic confusion. (D) $75°$ — computed $180 - 50 - 55$, misreading $\angle B$.
Q19 — Circle Equations Medium Answer: (B) $5$
Step 1: Group and complete the square for $x$: $x^2 - 6x \rightarrow (x-3)^2 - 9$
Step 2: Complete the square for $y$: $y^2 + 4y \rightarrow (y+2)^2 - 4$
Step 3: Substitute: $(x-3)^2 - 9 + (y+2)^2 - 4 = 12$
Step 4: $(x-3)^2 + (y+2)^2 = 12 + 9 + 4 = 25$
Step 5: Radius $= \sqrt{25} = 5$
Strategy: To complete the square for $x^2 + bx$: take half of $b$, square it, and add/subtract. The standard form $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$ immediately gives center $(h,k)$ and radius $r$.
Gotcha: (D) $25$ is $r^2$, not $r$. Always take the square root! (A) $3$ is the $x$-coordinate of the center.
Common mistakes: (C) $12$ — the original RHS, before completing the square. You must add $9$ and $4$ to get $25$.
Q20 — Right Triangle Trig Hard Answer: (C) $\dfrac{7}{5}$
Step 1: Find missing side $QR$: $QR = \sqrt{PR^2 - PQ^2} = \sqrt{225 - 81} = \sqrt{144} = 12$
Step 2: From angle $P$: opposite $= QR = 12$, adjacent $= PQ = 9$, hypotenuse $= PR = 15$
Step 3: $\sin P = 12/15 = 4/5$
Step 4: $\cos P = 9/15 = 3/5$
Step 5: $\sin P + \cos P = 4/5 + 3/5 = 7/5$
Strategy: SOH-CAH-TOA. Always identify which sides are opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse RELATIVE TO THE ANGLE in question. Use Pythagorean theorem first if a side is missing. Simplify fractions before adding.
Gotcha: (A) $7/15$ comes from writing $12/15 + 9/15 = 21/15$ but then reducing incorrectly. Actually $21/15 = 7/5$ — this IS the correct answer, so (A) is wrong because $7/15 \ne 21/15$. The trap is adding numerators without a common denominator.
Common mistakes: (A) $7/15$ — adding $12 + 9 = 21$ in numerator but keeping $15$ as denominator without simplifying $12/15$ and $9/15$ first (they DO have the same denominator, and $21/15 = 7/5$). (B) $1$ — a common misconception that $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ implies $\sin + \cos = 1$ (it doesn't!).
