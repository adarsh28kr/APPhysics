Kinematics (Q1-5)
Q1Answer: (C)
Velocity positive and decreasing for $0 < t < 2$, zero at $t=2$, negative for $t > 2$. At $t=4$, the particle is:
Key idea: Displacement = area under v-t curve. From $0$ to $2$: positive area (moves right). From $2$ to $4$: negative area (moves left). The positive area (0 to 2) is larger than the negative area (2 to 4) because v was decreasing — so the particle is still to the RIGHT.
At $t = 4$: $v < 0$, so it's moving LEFT.
Result: To the right of start, moving left.
AP strategy: This is a classic v-t graph question. Don't compute — think about areas. The integral of v(t) gives displacement. Positive area = rightward displacement, negative area = leftward. Compare areas geometrically.
Q2Answer: (B) $t = 6$ s
$a(t) = 6 - 2t$ m/s$^2$, starts from rest. When does $v = 0$ again?
Integrate: $v(t) = \int(6 - 2t)\,dt = 6t - t^2 + C$. Since $v(0) = 0$: $C = 0$.
Set $v = 0$: $6t - t^2 = t(6 - t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0$ or $t = 6$ s.
AP strategy: Factor before solving. $v(t) = t(6-t)$ immediately shows both roots. Velocity is positive between 0 and 6, peaking at $t = 3$.
Q3Answer: (E)
Ball A dropped, Ball B launched horizontally. Which is correct?
Time: Both have the same vertical motion (initial vertical velocity = 0 for both). Same height $\Rightarrow$ same time.
Speed: Ball A: $v_A = \sqrt{v_y^2} = v_y$. Ball B: $v_B = \sqrt{v_y^2 + v_0^2} > v_A$.
AP classic: Horizontal and vertical motions are independent. This appears on nearly every AP exam. The horizontal velocity doesn't affect the time to fall.
Q4Answer: (A) Quadruples
Range $R = v_0^2\sin(2\theta)/g$. If $v_0$ doubles, the range:
Direct: $R \propto v_0^2$. If $v_0 \to 2v_0$: $R \to 4R$. Range quadruples.
Gotcha: (B) "Doubles" = confused $R \propto v_0$ with $R \propto v_0^2$. Always check the power.
Q5Answer: (D) $t = 1$ s and $t = 3$ s
$x(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t$. Direction changes at:
Differentiate: $v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t^2 - 4t + 3) = 3(t-1)(t-3)$
$v = 0$ at $t = 1$ and $t = 3$. Sign changes at both (check: $v(0) = 9 > 0$, $v(2) = -3 < 0$, $v(4) = 9 > 0$).
AP strategy: "Changes direction" = $v = 0$ AND sign changes. Always verify the sign change — $v = 0$ alone could be a momentary stop without reversal.
Newton's Laws (Q6-12)
Q6Answer: (B) Equal
Truck collides with small car. The force of the truck on the car is:
Newton's 3rd Law: $F_{\text{truck on car}} = -F_{\text{car on truck}}$. Always equal magnitude, opposite direction.
AP classic: The #1 Newton's 3rd law misconception. The forces are ALWAYS equal. The car has a greater acceleration because it has less mass ($a = F/m$), but the forces are the same.
Q7Answer: (E) $\tan\theta$
Constant velocity down rough incline. $\mu_k = ?$
Constant velocity $\Rightarrow a = 0$: $mg\sin\theta = \mu_k mg\cos\theta$. Cancel $mg$: $\mu_k = \tan\theta$.
Q8Answer: (C) Greatest on $3m$, least on $m$
Three blocks ($m$, $2m$, $3m$) pushed by force $F$. Rank net forces.
All have same $a$: $a = F/(m + 2m + 3m) = F/(6m)$
Net force on each: $F_m = ma = F/6$, $F_{2m} = 2ma = F/3$, $F_{3m} = 3ma = F/2$.
Rank: $F_{3m} > F_{2m} > F_m$.
Key insight: Same acceleration $\neq$ same net force. $F_{net} = ma$, so the heavier block needs more net force to achieve the same $a$.
Q9Answer: (A) Decreases
Atwood machine: add $\Delta m$ to both sides. Acceleration:
$a' = \frac{(m_2 - m_1)g}{(m_1 + \Delta m) + (m_2 + \Delta m)} = \frac{(m_2 - m_1)g}{m_1 + m_2 + 2\Delta m}$
Numerator unchanged, denominator larger $\Rightarrow$ $a$ decreases.
Limiting case: As $\Delta m \to \infty$, $a \to 0$. Adding huge equal masses to both sides makes them nearly balanced. This confirms the answer.
Q10Answer: (D) Straight line eastward + falling
Ball on string in horizontal circle. String breaks when ball is at north moving east.
Newton's 1st Law: No string $\Rightarrow$ no centripetal force. Ball continues with its instantaneous velocity (tangential = eastward) and falls under gravity.
Path: Straight line east (horizontal) + parabolic fall (vertical). No curve, no spiral.
Gotcha: (B) "Northward" = confusing velocity direction (tangent) with radial direction. The ball moves in the direction it was GOING, not the direction toward/away from center.
Q11Answer: (B) 420 N
60 kg person, elevator accelerating down at 3 m/s$^2$. Scale reads:
$N = m(g - a) = 60(10 - 3) = 60 \times 7 = 420$ N
Quick rule: Accelerating down $\Rightarrow$ feel lighter ($N < mg$). Accelerating up $\Rightarrow$ feel heavier ($N > mg$).
Q12Answer: (E) $mg/b$
Drag $F_d = -bv$. Terminal velocity:
At terminal velocity: $a = 0 \Rightarrow mg = bv_t \Rightarrow v_t = mg/b$.
Gotcha: (D) $\sqrt{mg/b}$ = confused with QUADRATIC drag ($mg = cv^2$). For linear drag, $v_t = mg/b$ (no square root).
Work, Energy & Power (Q13-18)
Q13Answer: (C) 9 J
$F(x) = 4x - x^2$. Work from $x = 0$ to $x = 3$:
$W = \int_0^3 (4x - x^2)\,dx = \left[2x^2 - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3 = (18 - 9) - 0 = 9$ J
Q14Answer: (A) $5R/2$
Block enters loop of radius $R$. Minimum launch height $h$:
At top of loop (barely maintains contact): $N = 0 \Rightarrow mg = mv^2_{top}/R \Rightarrow v^2_{top} = gR$
Energy from $h$ to top of loop (height $= 2R$): $mgh = mg(2R) + \frac{1}{2}mv^2_{top} = mg(2R) + \frac{1}{2}m(gR)$
$h = 2R + R/2 = 5R/2$
AP classic: This combines energy conservation with circular motion. The trick is knowing that "barely maintains contact" means $N = 0$ at the top, which gives $v^2 = gR$.
Q15Answer: (D) $x = \pm\sqrt{b/(2a)}$
$U(x) = ax^4 - bx^2$. Stable equilibrium at:
Equilibrium: $F = -dU/dx = -(4ax^3 - 2bx) = 0 \Rightarrow x(4ax^2 - 2b) = 0$
Solutions: $x = 0$ or $x^2 = b/(2a) \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{b/(2a)}$
Stability test: $d^2U/dx^2 = 12ax^2 - 2b$. At $x = 0$: $-2b < 0$ (unstable). At $x = \pm\sqrt{b/(2a)}$: $12a \cdot b/(2a) - 2b = 4b > 0$ (stable ✓).
AP strategy: Equilibrium = $F = 0$ ($dU/dx = 0$). Stable = minimum of $U$ ($d^2U/dx^2 > 0$). Unstable = maximum of $U$ ($d^2U/dx^2 < 0$).
Q16Answer: (B) 40 N
5 kg at 8 m/s, brought to rest over 4 m. Friction force:
Work-energy: $-f \cdot d = 0 - \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. $f = \frac{mv^2}{2d} = \frac{5 \times 64}{2 \times 4} = \frac{320}{8} = 40$ N.
Q17Answer: (A) $mgv\sin\theta$
Car at constant $v$ up hill angle $\theta$. Power:
At constant velocity: engine force = gravity component = $mg\sin\theta$. $P = Fv = mgv\sin\theta$.
Gotcha: (E) "$0$ because constant velocity" is wrong. Constant velocity means zero NET force, but the engine still does work against gravity.
Q18Answer: (C) $A/2$
Spring oscillation: KE = 3 PE. Find $|x|$:
$KE + PE = E_{total}$. If $KE = 3PE$: $4PE = E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2$. So $PE = \frac{1}{8}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$.
$x^2 = A^2/4 \Rightarrow |x| = A/2$.
Momentum (Q19-21)
Q19Answer: (E) Same magnitude of momentum
Two skaters ($M$ and $2M$) push off from rest. Afterward:
Conservation of momentum: $p_{total} = 0$ (started from rest). So $p_1 = -p_2$. Equal magnitude, opposite direction.
NOT same speed: $Mv_1 = 2Mv_2 \Rightarrow v_1 = 2v_2$. Light skater goes twice as fast.
NOT same KE: $KE_1 = \frac{1}{2}Mv_1^2 = \frac{1}{2}M(2v_2)^2 = 2Mv_2^2$. $KE_2 = \frac{1}{2}(2M)v_2^2 = Mv_2^2$. Light skater has double the KE.
Q20Answer: (D) 2 m/s
2 kg at rest, $F(t) = 12t^2$ for $0 \le t \le 1$. Speed at $t = 1$:
$J = \int_0^1 12t^2\,dt = \left[4t^3\right]_0^1 = 4$ N·s
$v = J/m = 4/2 = 2$ m/s
Q21Answer: (A) Inelastic
Ball $m$ at speed $v$ hits identical ball at rest. After: first ball at $v/2$ at 60°. Collision type:
Momentum is ALWAYS conserved (in all collisions). So "momentum is conserved" doesn't tell you if it's elastic.
Check KE: Initial $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. First ball after: $\frac{1}{2}m(v/2)^2 = mv^2/8$. By momentum conservation the second ball also has KE, but the total will be less than $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$. The collision is inelastic.
AP strategy: "Elastic" means KE conserved. "Momentum conserved" is true for ALL collisions (no external forces). Don't confuse the two!
Rotation (Q22-27)
Q22Answer: (B) Solid sphere
Rolling race: solid sphere, hollow sphere, solid cylinder, hollow cylinder.
For rolling without slipping: $a = g\sin\theta/(1 + I/(mR^2))$. Smaller $I/(mR^2)$ = faster.
$I/(mR^2)$ values: Solid sphere: $2/5$. Solid cylinder: $1/2$. Hollow sphere: $2/3$. Hollow cylinder: $1$.
$2/5 < 1/2 < 2/3 < 1$. Solid sphere wins.
Memorize the ranking: Solid sphere > solid cylinder > hollow sphere > hollow cylinder. Objects with mass concentrated near the center roll faster (less "rotational inertia tax").
Q23Answer: (C) $1/2$
Disk ($I$, $\omega$) + identical disk at rest. Fraction of KE lost:
Angular momentum conserved: $I\omega = 2I\omega_f \Rightarrow \omega_f = \omega/2$
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$. $KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(2I)(\omega/2)^2 = \frac{1}{4}I\omega^2$.
Fraction lost $= (KE_i - KE_f)/KE_i = 1/2$.
Q24Answer: (D) $6g/(5L)$
Uniform rod ($M$, $L$) pivoted at one end, mass $M/2$ at other end. Initial $\alpha$:
$I$: $I_{rod} = ML^2/3$. $I_{mass} = (M/2)L^2 = ML^2/2$. Total $= 5ML^2/6$.
Torque: $\tau_{rod} = Mg(L/2)$. $\tau_{mass} = (M/2)gL$. Total $= MgL$.
$\alpha = \tau/I = MgL/(5ML^2/6) = 6g/(5L)$
Q25Answer: (E) $7ML^2/48$
Rod $I_{cm} = ML^2/12$. Axis at $L/4$ from one end ($= L/4$ from center):
Parallel axis theorem: $I = I_{cm} + Md^2 = ML^2/12 + M(L/4)^2 = ML^2/12 + ML^2/16$
$= \frac{4ML^2 + 3ML^2}{48} = \frac{7ML^2}{48}$
Q26Answer: (A) 2 J
Solid disk, 2 kg, radius 1 m, $\omega = 2$ rad/s. Rotational KE:
$I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2)(1) = 1$ kg·m$^2$. $KE = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1)(4) = 2$ J.
Q27Answer: (C) Doubles
Skater halves her $I$. KE:
$L$ conserved: $I\omega = (I/2)\omega' \Rightarrow \omega' = 2\omega$.
$KE' = \frac{1}{2}(I/2)(2\omega)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(I/2)(4\omega^2) = I\omega^2 = 2 \times \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = 2KE$.
Where does the extra energy come from? The skater does internal work (muscles) to pull her arms in against centripetal "force." This converts chemical energy to rotational KE.
Oscillations & Gravitation (Q28-30)
Q28Answer: (B) $-\omega^2 A$
$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)$. Acceleration at $t = 0$:
$a(t) = -\omega^2 A\cos(\omega t)$. At $t = 0$: $a = -\omega^2 A\cos(0) = -\omega^2 A$.
Maximum magnitude, directed toward equilibrium (negative = restoring).
Q29Answer: (D) $-GMm/(2r)$
Satellite in circular orbit of radius $r$. Total energy:
$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. For circular orbit: $\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{2r}$
$E = KE + PE = \frac{GMm}{2r} - \frac{GMm}{r} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$
Key result: For circular orbits, $KE = -E$ and $PE = 2E$. The total energy is always negative (bound orbit) and equals half the potential energy.
Q30Answer: (E) $T/2$
Pendulum period $T$. On planet with $g' = 4g$:
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. $T' = 2\pi\sqrt{L/(4g)} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2\pi\sqrt{L/g} = T/2$.
Cross-Topic / Integrated (Q31-35)
Q31Answer: (A) $7v^2/(10g)$
Solid sphere rolls up incline. Maximum height:
Total KE (translational + rotational): $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2}{5}mr^2\right)\left(\frac{v}{r}\right)^2 = \frac{7}{10}mv^2$
Set equal to $mgh$: $h = 7v^2/(10g)$.
Gotcha: (B) $v^2/(2g)$ = forgot rotational KE. This would be correct only for a sliding (non-rolling) object.
Q32Answer: (C) Inelastic
3 kg at 4 m/s hits 1 kg at rest. After: 3 kg at 3 m/s.
Momentum: $3(4) = 3(3) + 1(v_2) \Rightarrow v_2 = 3$ m/s.
KE check: $KE_i = \frac{1}{2}(3)(16) = 24$ J. $KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(3)(9) + \frac{1}{2}(1)(9) = 13.5 + 4.5 = 18$ J.
$24 \neq 18$: KE is NOT conserved, but carts separate. Inelastic (not perfectly inelastic).
Q33Answer: (B) Less than $K$
Block up rough incline and back. Final KE:
Friction does negative work BOTH ways (up and down). Total energy lost $= 2\mu_k mgd\cos\theta > 0$.
Final $KE = K - W_{friction} < K$.
Q34Answer: (D) $\sqrt{L/g}$
Period depends on $L$ and $g$. By dimensional analysis:
$[T] = $ s. $[\sqrt{L/g}] = \sqrt{\text{m}/(\text{m/s}^2)} = \sqrt{s^2} = s$ ✓
All other choices have wrong dimensions.
Q35Answer: (E)
Tunnel through Earth. Period depends on:
Inside uniform Earth: $F = -\frac{4\pi G\rho m}{3}x = -kx$ (SHM). $\omega = \sqrt{4\pi G\rho/3}$.
Period depends on $G$ and $\rho$ (Earth's density) only. Independent of object mass, drop depth, or tunnel diameter.
Fun fact: The period is $T \approx 84$ min — the same as a low-Earth orbit! This is not a coincidence; both depend on $\sqrt{R^3/(GM_E)} = \sqrt{3/(4\pi G\rho)}$.
Section II: Free Response Solutions
FRQ 1: Atwood Machine on an Incline15 points
$M = 4.0$ kg on rough 37° incline ($\mu_k = 0.25$), connected to $m = 3.0$ kg hanging. $g = 10$ m/s$^2$, $\sin 37° = 0.6$, $\cos 37° = 0.8$.
(a) Free-body diagrams (3 pts)
Block $M$ (incline): Weight $Mg$ (down), Normal $N$ (perpendicular to incline), Tension $T$ (up the incline), Friction $f_k$ (down the incline, opposing upward motion).
Block $m$ (hanging): Weight $mg$ (down), Tension $T$ (up).
Scoring: 1 pt each correct FBD (all forces labeled with correct directions), 1 pt for friction direction consistent with motion.
(b) Acceleration and tension (4 pts)
Check: does $m$ fall or does $M$ slide down? $mg = 30$ N. $Mg\sin 37° = 24$ N. Since $30 > 24$, the hanging block falls and $M$ goes UP the incline.
Friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = \mu_k Mg\cos 37° = 0.25(4)(10)(0.8) = 8$ N (down the incline, opposing upward motion).
System equation: $mg - Mg\sin 37° - f_k = (M + m)a$
$30 - 24 - 8 = 7a \Rightarrow -2 = 7a$
Wait — $a = -2/7 \approx -0.29$ m/s$^2$. Negative means the system doesn't move as assumed! Let's check: if $M$ tries to slide DOWN, friction acts UP the incline. Then: $Mg\sin 37° - mg - f_k = (M+m)a \Rightarrow 24 - 30 - 8 = 7a \Rightarrow -14 = 7a \Rightarrow a = -2$. Still negative.
The system doesn't move! Static friction holds it. Check: net force without friction = $30 - 24 = 6$ N. Max static friction $\geq f_k$. Since we're told $\mu_k = 0.25$, and typically $\mu_s > \mu_k$, the max static friction is at least 8 N $> 6$ N. So $a = 0$ and $T = mg = 30$ N (or $T = Mg\sin 37° + f_s = 24 + 6 = 30$ N).
Result: $a = 0$, $T = 30$ N, $f_s = 6$ N.
Scoring: 1 pt friction force, 1 pt system equation, 1 pt recognizing system may not move (or correctly computing $a$), 1 pt tension.
(c) Speed after 2 m using work-energy (3 pts)
Since $a = 0$ from part (b), the system doesn't move, so the speed after any distance is $v = 0$.
Alternatively, if the student found $a > 0$: $W_{net} = \Delta KE$. $mg(2) - Mg\sin 37°(2) - f_k(2) = \frac{1}{2}(M+m)v^2$. With $a = 0$, all work terms cancel: $v = 0$.
Scoring: 1 pt work-energy setup, 1 pt correct calculation, 1 pt consistent with part (b).
(d) Variable force $F(t) = 20 + 10t$, derive $v(t)$ (5 pts)
Net force on $M$ up the incline: $F(t) - Mg\sin 37° - f_k = Ma(t)$
$(20 + 10t) - 24 - 8 = 4a(t) \Rightarrow a(t) = (10t - 12)/4$
Note: For $t < 1.2$ s, $a < 0$. But the block starts from rest and can't move backward (force is up the incline), so $v = 0$ until $F$ overcomes friction + gravity. The block starts moving when $F = Mg\sin 37° + f_k = 32$ N, i.e., $20 + 10t = 32 \Rightarrow t = 1.2$ s.
For $t \geq 1.2$ s: $v(t) = \int_{1.2}^{t} \frac{10t' - 12}{4}\,dt' = \frac{1}{4}\left[5t'^2 - 12t'\right]_{1.2}^{t}$
$= \frac{1}{4}\left[(5t^2 - 12t) - (5(1.44) - 14.4)\right] = \frac{1}{4}\left[5t^2 - 12t - 7.2 + 14.4\right] = \frac{5t^2 - 12t + 7.2}{4}$
At $t = 2$ s: $v(2) = \frac{5(4) - 12(2) + 7.2}{4} = \frac{20 - 24 + 7.2}{4} = \frac{3.2}{4} = 0.8$ m/s
Scoring: 1 pt net force equation, 1 pt recognizing threshold time, 1 pt correct integration limits, 1 pt correct $v(t)$, 1 pt numerical answer.
FRQ 2: Turntable and Ball Collision15 points
Disk $M = 8.0$ kg, $R = 0.50$ m, initially at rest. Ball $m = 0.40$ kg, $v_0 = 10$ m/s thrown tangentially at rim, sticks.
(a) Moments of inertia (2 pts)
$I_{disk} = \frac{1}{2}MR^2 = \frac{1}{2}(8)(0.25) = 1.0$ kg·m$^2$
$I_{ball} = mR^2 = (0.40)(0.25) = 0.10$ kg·m$^2$
(b) Angular velocity after collision (4 pts)
Angular momentum before: $L_i = mv_0 R = (0.40)(10)(0.50) = 2.0$ kg·m$^2$/s
After (ball + disk rotate together): $L_f = (I_{disk} + I_{ball})\omega = 1.10\omega$
Conservation: $2.0 = 1.10\omega \Rightarrow \omega = 2.0/1.10 \approx 1.82$ rad/s
Scoring: 1 pt angular momentum of ball, 1 pt total $I$ after, 1 pt conservation equation, 1 pt answer.
(c) Fraction of KE lost (4 pts)
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.40)(100) = 20$ J
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(1.10)(1.82)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.10)(3.31) \approx 1.82$ J
Fraction lost $= (20 - 1.82)/20 \approx 0.91$ or $91\%$.
Lost energy goes to heat, sound, and deformation at the contact point.
Scoring: 1 pt $KE_i$, 1 pt $KE_f$, 1 pt fraction, 1 pt explaining where energy goes.
(d) Ball bounces off at 2 m/s opposite direction (5 pts)
Angular momentum: $L_i = mv_0 R = 2.0$ kg·m$^2$/s (same as before).
After bounce: Ball moves at 2 m/s in opposite tangential direction. $L_{ball,f} = m(-v_{bounce})R = (0.40)(-2)(0.50) = -0.40$ kg·m$^2$/s
Conservation: $2.0 = I_{disk}\omega + (-0.40) \Rightarrow 1.0\omega = 2.40 \Rightarrow \omega = 2.40$ rad/s
KE comparison: $KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(1.0)(2.40)^2 + \frac{1}{2}(0.40)(2)^2 = 2.88 + 0.80 = 3.68$ J
Fraction lost $= (20 - 3.68)/20 = 0.816$ or $81.6\%$. LESS energy is lost in the bounce compared to sticking (91%).
Scoring: 1 pt ball's angular momentum after bounce (with correct sign), 1 pt conservation equation, 1 pt $\omega$, 1 pt $KE_f$, 1 pt comparison with justification.
FRQ 3: Spring Oscillation — Theory & Experiment15 points
Cart of mass $m$ on frictionless air track, attached to spring with constant $k$.
(a) Derive equation of motion (3 pts)
Newton's 2nd law: $F = ma \Rightarrow -kx = m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \Rightarrow \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\frac{k}{m}x$
Verify solution: Let $x = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. Then $\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 A\cos(\omega t + \phi) = -\omega^2 x$.
This matches the equation if $\omega^2 = k/m$, i.e., $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$. ✓
Scoring: 1 pt $F = -kx = ma$, 1 pt rearranging to standard form, 1 pt verifying solution with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$.
(b) Write $x(t)$, $v(t)$, $a(t)$ with numbers (3 pts)
$\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{8/0.5} = \sqrt{16} = 4$ rad/s. $A = 0.20$ m. Released from rest at max displacement: $\phi = 0$.
$x(t) = 0.20\cos(4t)$ m
$v(t) = -0.80\sin(4t)$ m/s
$a(t) = -3.2\cos(4t)$ m/s$^2$
Scoring: 1 pt each for correct $x$, $v$, $a$ with correct numerical coefficients.
(c) Graph and $k/m$ from data (4 pts)
Relationship: $v_{max} = \omega A = A\sqrt{k/m}$. So $v_{max}$ is proportional to $A$.
Plot: $v_{max}$ (y-axis) vs $A$ (x-axis). This gives a straight line through the origin.
Slope: $= \omega = \sqrt{k/m}$. From data: slope $= 0.20/0.05 = 4.0$ rad/s.
Therefore: $k/m = \omega^2 = 16$ s$^{-2}$.
Scoring: 1 pt identifying $v_{max} = \omega A$, 1 pt correct graph axes, 1 pt slope = $\omega$, 1 pt $k/m = 16$.
(d) Find $\mu_k$ from amplitude decay (5 pts)
Given: $\Delta A = 2\mu_k mg/k$ per half-cycle. Amplitude goes from 0.20 m to 0 in 10 half-cycles.
Total amplitude lost: $10 \times \Delta A = 0.20$ m. So $\Delta A = 0.020$ m per half-cycle.
Solve: $0.020 = \frac{2\mu_k mg}{k} = \frac{2\mu_k (0.50)(10)}{8} = \frac{10\mu_k}{8} = 1.25\mu_k$
$\mu_k = 0.020/1.25 = 0.016$
Scoring: 1 pt $\Delta A$ per half-cycle, 1 pt total amplitude equation, 1 pt substituting values, 1 pt solving for $\mu_k$, 1 pt correct numerical answer.
