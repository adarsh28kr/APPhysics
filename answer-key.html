Multiple Choice: Detailed Solutions

Kinematics (Q1-6)

Question 1 Kinematics
Answer: (A) 0 m
Displacement $= \int_0^6 (4t - t^2)\,dt = \left[2t^2 - \frac{t^3}{3}\right]_0^6 = 2(36) - \frac{216}{3} = 72 - 72 = 0$ m
Common mistake: Students who get 12 m likely computed the distance (absolute value of displacement in each interval) rather than the net displacement. Others get 36 by forgetting to integrate and just plugging $t=6$ into $v(t)$.
Question 2 Kinematics
Answer: (C) $3H/4$
At max height: $v_0^2 = 2gH$, so $H = v_0^2/(2g)$.
When speed = $v_0/2$: $v^2 = v_0^2 - 2gh \Rightarrow (v_0/2)^2 = v_0^2 - 2gh$
$v_0^2/4 = v_0^2 - 2gh \Rightarrow 2gh = 3v_0^2/4 \Rightarrow h = 3v_0^2/(8g) = 3H/4$
Common mistake: Choosing $H/2$ — this confuses "half the speed" with "half the height." Energy is proportional to $v^2$, so half speed means 3/4 of the KE has converted to PE.
Question 3 Kinematics
Answer: (C) $g$, directed downward
The acceleration due to gravity is constant throughout projectile motion: $\vec{a} = -g\hat{j}$ everywhere, including at the top. The velocity is purely horizontal at the top, but acceleration is always $g$ downward.
Common mistake: Choosing (A) $0$ — students confuse the fact that the vertical velocity is zero at the top with the acceleration being zero. Others choose (E) thinking of centripetal acceleration formula.
Question 4 Kinematics
Answer: (C) $t = 2$ s and $t = 4$ s
$v(t) = dx/dt = 3t^2 - 18t + 24 = 3(t^2 - 6t + 8) = 3(t-2)(t-4) = 0$
Solutions: $t = 2$ s and $t = 4$ s.
Common mistake: Setting $x(t) = 0$ instead of $v(t) = 0$. "At rest" means zero velocity, not zero position.
Question 5 Kinematics
Answer: (C) They hit the ground at the same time
Both objects have the same initial vertical velocity (zero) and experience the same vertical acceleration ($g$). Horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Time to fall depends only on height and vertical initial velocity.
Common mistake: Choosing (A) because "shorter path" seems intuitive, or (D) thinking horizontal motion somehow "uses up" time. This tests understanding of independence of perpendicular motions.
Question 6 Kinematics
Answer: (C) 9 m/s
Maximum velocity occurs when $a(t) = 0$: $6 - 2t = 0 \Rightarrow t = 3$ s.
$v = \int_0^3 (6-2t)\,dt = [6t - t^2]_0^3 = 18 - 9 = 9$ m/s
Common mistake: Getting 6 m/s by evaluating $a(0) \times t$ (not recognizing non-constant acceleration requires integration). Getting 18 by forgetting the $-t^2$ term.

Newton's Laws (Q7-12)

Question 7 Newton's Laws
Answer: (C) $mg\tan\theta$
For equilibrium on frictionless incline with horizontal force $F$:
Along incline: $F\cos\theta = mg\sin\theta$
$F = mg\tan\theta$
(Normal to incline: $N = mg\cos\theta + F\sin\theta$)
Common mistake: Choosing $mg\sin\theta$ — this would be correct if the force were along the incline, but it's horizontal. Must decompose $F$ along and perpendicular to the incline.
Question 8 Newton's Laws
Answer: (D) 60 N
For the 5 kg block not to slide, friction must provide its acceleration: $f_s = m_{top} \cdot a$
Maximum static friction: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s m_{top} g = 0.4 \times 5 \times 10 = 20$ N
Maximum acceleration: $a_{max} = f_{s,max}/m_{top} = 20/5 = 4$ m/s$^2$
Both blocks must have this acceleration: $F = (m_{top} + m_{bottom}) \times a_{max} = 15 \times 4 = 60$ N
Common mistake: Getting 20 N by computing $\mu mg$ for the top block only, forgetting that $F$ accelerates both blocks. Getting 40 N by applying friction force to just the bottom block mass.
Question 9 Newton's Laws
Answer: (D) $5Mg/4$
Newton's second law: $T - Mg = Ma$
$T = M(g + a) = M(g + g/4) = 5Mg/4$
Common mistake: Getting $3Mg/4$ by writing $T = M(g-a)$ (sign error — this would be for downward acceleration). The tension must exceed $Mg$ for upward acceleration.
Question 10 Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $x = 0$ and $x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$
Equilibrium: $F(x) = 0 \Rightarrow -kx + bx^3 = 0 \Rightarrow x(-k + bx^2) = 0$
Solutions: $x = 0$ or $x^2 = k/b \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$
Common mistake: Only finding $x = 0$ by dividing both sides by $x$ (losing solutions). Note: stability analysis shows $x = 0$ is stable, $x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$ are unstable (check $dF/dx$).
Question 11 Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g}$
For conical pendulum, radius $r = L\sin\theta$.
Vertical: $T\cos\theta = mg$
Horizontal: $T\sin\theta = m\omega^2 r = m\omega^2 L\sin\theta$
Dividing: $\cos\theta = g/(\omega^2 L) \Rightarrow \omega^2 = g/(L\cos\theta)$
Period $= 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g}$
Common mistake: Getting $2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ by confusing with simple pendulum. The conical pendulum period depends on the angle.
Question 12 Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$
Along incline (positive = down): $mg\sin\theta - f_k = ma$
Normal: $N = mg\cos\theta$, so $f_k = \mu_k mg\cos\theta$
$a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$
Common mistake: Adding friction instead of subtracting (getting choice A) — friction opposes motion, which is down the incline, so friction acts up the incline.

Work, Energy & Power (Q13-18)

Question 13 Work & Energy
Answer: (B) 18 J
$W = \int_1^3 (3x^2 - 2x)\,dx = [x^3 - x^2]_1^3 = (27-9) - (1-1) = 18 - 0 = 18$ J.
Common mistake: Evaluating $F(3) \times (3-1) = (27-6)(2) = 42$ — using the final force times displacement instead of integrating. Must use $W = \int F\,dx$ for variable forces.
Question 14 Work & Energy
Answer: (C) $\sqrt{2g(h - \mu_k d)}$
Energy conservation with friction: $mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \mu_k mg d$
$v = \sqrt{2g(h - \mu_k d)}$
This is equivalent to $\sqrt{2gh - 2\mu_k gd}$ which is choice (B) written differently — both are the same expression.
Common mistake: Forgetting the factor of 2 in front of $\mu_k gd$ (choice A), or squaring $d$ incorrectly (choice E). Also: choice (D) subtracts $\mu_k gd$ outside the square root — dimensionally wrong.
Question 15 Work & Energy
Answer: (A) $x = 0$ only
Equilibrium: $F = -dU/dx = -(8x - 4x^3) = 0 \Rightarrow 4x(2-x^2)=0$
Solutions: $x = 0, \pm\sqrt{2}$
Stability check: $d^2U/dx^2 = 8 - 12x^2$.
At $x=0$: $d^2U/dx^2 = 8 > 0$ → local minimum of $U$ → stable.
At $x=\pm\sqrt{2}$: $d^2U/dx^2 = 8-24 = -16 < 0$ → local maximum of $U$ → unstable.
Only $x=0$ is a position of stable equilibrium.
Common mistake: Finding all equilibrium points ($x = 0, \pm\sqrt{2}$) and selecting (C) without checking stability. You must compute $d^2U/dx^2$ to determine which are stable (local minima of $U$) vs unstable (local maxima). This is a classic calculus trap on the AP exam.
Question 16 Work & Energy
Answer: (C) $kd^2/(2mg)$
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}kd^2 = mgh$
$h = kd^2/(2mg)$
Common mistake: Forgetting the $1/2$ in spring PE (getting choice B: $kd^2/mg$), or using $kd$ instead of $kd^2$ (choice A). The spring stores energy as $\frac{1}{2}kx^2$, not $kx$.
Question 17 Work & Energy
Answer: (D) 6000 W
$P = Fv = mgv = 200 \times 10 \times 3 = 6000$ W
(Using $g \approx 10$ m/s$^2$ as is standard for AP Physics)
Common mistake: Using $g = 9.8$ gives 5880 W (not a choice, so students should recognize $g = 10$ is expected). Getting 600 W by forgetting to multiply by $g$.
Question 18 Work & Energy
Answer: (B) $\sqrt{20}$ m/s
Work = area of triangle = $\frac{1}{2}(10)(4) = 20$ J
Work-energy theorem: $W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - 0$
$20 = \frac{1}{2}(2)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 20 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}$ m/s
Note: choices (B) and (C) are the same value written differently.
Common mistake: Using $F \times d = 10 \times 4 = 40$ J (rectangle instead of triangle). Must read the graph carefully — it's a triangle, so area = $\frac{1}{2}$ base $\times$ height.

Momentum & Collisions (Q19-24)

Question 19 Momentum
Answer: (B) 1 m/s in the opposite direction
Elastic collision formulas:
$v_1' = \frac{m_1 - m_2}{m_1 + m_2}v_1 = \frac{2-3}{5}(5) = -1$ m/s
Negative means opposite direction. Speed = 1 m/s opposite to original.
Common mistake: Using inelastic collision formula (just momentum conservation) gives $v = 2$ m/s for combined mass. Must use both momentum AND energy conservation for elastic collisions.
Question 20 Momentum
Answer: (B) $v\sqrt{2}/3$
Conservation of momentum (initially at rest, total $p = 0$):
$\vec{p}_1 + \vec{p}_2 + \vec{p}_3 = 0$
Two pieces perpendicular, each momentum $= mv$:
$|\vec{p}_1 + \vec{p}_2| = \sqrt{(mv)^2 + (mv)^2} = mv\sqrt{2}$
$p_3 = 3m \cdot v_3 = mv\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow v_3 = v\sqrt{2}/3$
Common mistake: Adding momenta algebraically ($2mv$) instead of vectorially. Since the pieces are perpendicular, use Pythagorean theorem.
Question 21 Momentum
Answer: (C) $2mv\cos\theta$
The component of velocity parallel to the wall is unchanged. The component perpendicular to the wall reverses.
$\Delta p = 2m(v\cos\theta)$ (perpendicular component reverses)
Impulse $= |\Delta \vec{p}| = 2mv\cos\theta$
Common mistake: Getting $2mv$ by ignoring the angle. Or $2mv\sin\theta$ by confusing which component reverses. The angle is measured from the normal, so the perpendicular component is $v\cos\theta$.
Question 22 Momentum
Answer: (B) $v_e \ln 2$
Tsiolkovsky rocket equation: $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/M_f)$
With $M_f = M_0/2$: $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/(M_0/2)) = v_e \ln 2$
Common mistake: Writing $v_e \times (M_0 - M_f)/M_0 = v_e/2$ (linearizing instead of using the logarithmic relationship). The rocket equation involves a logarithm because the mass changes continuously.
Question 23 Momentum
Answer: (C) $3R/8$
This is a standard result. Using integration with disk elements:
$\bar{z} = \frac{\int_0^R z \cdot \pi(R^2-z^2)\rho\,dz}{\frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 \rho} = \frac{3}{8}R$
Common mistake: Confusing with hemisphere shell ($R/2$) or half-sphere volume centroid formulas. This is one to memorize for the exam.
Question 24 Momentum
Answer: (C) 6 m/s
Perfectly inelastic: $m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1+m_2)v_f$
$1(10) + 2(4) = 3v_f \Rightarrow v_f = 18/3 = 6$ m/s
Common mistake: Averaging speeds $(10+4)/2 = 7$ (ignoring unequal masses). Must weight by mass.

Rotation (Q25-30)

Question 25 Rotation
Answer: (C) $(2/3)g\sin\theta$
For rolling without slipping: $a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + I/(MR^2)}$
Solid disk: $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$, so $I/(MR^2) = 1/2$
$a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + 1/2} = \frac{2}{3}g\sin\theta$
Common mistake: Getting $g\sin\theta$ by ignoring rotational inertia (treating it as sliding). Getting $(5/7)g\sin\theta$ by using the moment of inertia for a solid sphere instead of a disk.
Question 26 Rotation
Answer: (C) $\sqrt{3g/L}$
Energy conservation: PE lost = KE gained
CM falls $L/2$: $Mg(L/2) = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
For rod about end: $I = ML^2/3$
$Mg(L/2) = \frac{1}{2}(ML^2/3)\omega^2$
$\omega = \sqrt{3g/L}$
Common mistake: Using $I = ML^2/12$ (center pivot instead of end). Or having CM fall by $L$ instead of $L/2$. The pivot is at one end, so CM is at $L/2$ from pivot and falls that distance.
Question 27 Rotation
Answer: (A) The solid sphere arrives first and has greater speed
$a = g\sin\theta/(1 + I/(MR^2))$
Solid sphere: $I = 2MR^2/5$, factor = $1 + 2/5 = 7/5$, $a = 5g\sin\theta/7$
Hollow shell: $I = 2MR^2/3$, factor = $1 + 2/3 = 5/3$, $a = 3g\sin\theta/5$
Since $5/7 > 3/5$, solid sphere has greater acceleration, arrives first, and has greater speed.
Common mistake: Thinking same mass/radius means same behavior. The distribution of mass matters — more mass concentrated far from axis (shell) means more rotational inertia and slower rolling.
Question 28 Rotation
Answer: (B) Increases by a factor of 3
Angular momentum conserved: $L = I\omega_0 = (I/3)\omega_f \Rightarrow \omega_f = 3\omega_0$
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_0^2$
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(I/3)(3\omega_0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(I/3)(9\omega_0^2) = \frac{3}{2}I\omega_0^2 = 3 \times KE_i$
Common mistake: Thinking energy is conserved (choice A). The skater does internal work by pulling arms in. Or getting factor of 9 by only tracking $\omega$ increase without accounting for reduced $I$.
Question 29 Rotation
Answer: (C) $1/2$
Angular momentum conserved: $I\omega = 2I\omega_f \Rightarrow \omega_f = \omega/2$
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(2I)(\omega/2)^2 = \frac{1}{4}I\omega^2$
Fraction lost $= (KE_i - KE_f)/KE_i = 1 - 1/2 = 1/2$
Common mistake: Thinking no energy is lost because angular momentum is conserved. Angular momentum conservation does NOT imply energy conservation when internal friction does work.
Question 30 Rotation
Answer: (A) $\tau_0 T/I$
$\omega = \frac{1}{I}\int_0^\infty \tau_0 e^{-t/T}\,dt = \frac{\tau_0}{I}[-T e^{-t/T}]_0^\infty = \frac{\tau_0}{I}(0-(-T)) = \frac{\tau_0 T}{I}$
Common mistake: Forgetting the $T$ factor from integrating the exponential. $\int e^{-t/T}dt = -Te^{-t/T}$, not just $-e^{-t/T}$. This is a calculus trap — must know how to integrate exponentials.

Oscillations & Gravitation (Q31-35)

Question 31 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (B) $\sqrt{3kA^2/(4m)}$
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
At $x = A/2$: $\frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}k(A/2)^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
$kA^2 - kA^2/4 = mv^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = \frac{3kA^2}{4m}$
$v = \sqrt{3kA^2/(4m)} = \frac{A}{2}\sqrt{3k/m}$
Common mistake: Using $v = \omega A$ (max speed) at all positions. Or getting $\sqrt{kA^2/(4m)}$ by subtracting wrong term. Remember: $v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}$.
Question 32 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (D) $T\sqrt{2}$
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. On new planet: $g' = GM'/R'^2 = G(2M)/(2R)^2 = 2GM/(4R^2) = g/2$
$T' = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g'} = 2\pi\sqrt{2L/g} = T\sqrt{2}$
Common mistake: Thinking twice the mass means twice the gravity (forgetting radius is also doubled and appears squared). Or getting $T/\sqrt{2}$ by inverting the ratio.
Question 33 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (C) Escape Earth's gravitational field
Circular orbit speed: $v_c = \sqrt{GM/r}$. Escape speed: $v_e = \sqrt{2GM/r} = v_c\sqrt{2}$.
If speed is increased by factor $\sqrt{2}$: new speed $= v_c\sqrt{2} = v_e$.
The satellite reaches exactly escape velocity and will escape to infinity.
Common mistake: Thinking $\sqrt{2}$ times the speed means a circular orbit of twice the radius (confusing $v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$ with escape). This is exactly the escape condition.
Question 34 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (C) $2\pi\sqrt{2L/(3g)}$
Physical pendulum: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{I/(mgd)}$
Rod pivoted at end: $I = ML^2/3$, distance to CM: $d = L/2$
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{ML^2/3}{Mg(L/2)}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2L}{3g}}$
Common mistake: Using simple pendulum formula $2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ (treating rod as point mass). Or using $d = L$ instead of $d = L/2$ for the CM distance.
Question 35 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (B) $\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k(m_1+m_2)}{m_1 m_2}}$
Two-body oscillation uses reduced mass: $\mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$
$f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{k/\mu} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k(m_1+m_2)}{m_1 m_2}}$
Note: choices (B) and (C) are equivalent since $m_1m_2/(m_1+m_2) = \mu$.
Common mistake: Using total mass $m_1+m_2$ instead of reduced mass (choice A). The reduced mass accounts for both bodies moving; the total mass formula would only apply if one mass were infinite (fixed wall).

Wait — at the top of a quarter circle, the center is below, so: $N + mg = mv^2/R$ if the track curves over, or the setup may differ. With quarter-circle from bottom, the "top" has center at the bottom. At the top: $mg + N = mv^2/R$ (centripetal toward center = downward)... Actually for a quarter circle where the block goes from horizontal up to vertical height $R$, at the top the block moves horizontally and the center of curvature is below. So $mg + N$ doesn't work — actually at the top of the arc, $N$ points inward (down) if the track is concave down, but for a quarter circle going up, at the top the track curves the block — we need more context. Final expression: $N = \frac{kd^2}{mR} - 2mg - mg = \frac{kd^2}{mR} - 3mg$ (if like a loop, center above) OR $N = \frac{kd^2}{mR} - mg$ for quarter-pipe where normal is radial and weight is tangential at the top. For quarter-circle ending at height $R$ with horizontal velocity: at the top, the center is directly below, so $N - mg = mv^2/R$ would apply if on outside. Standard: For inside of quarter-pipe at the top, centripetal direction is downward: $mg + N_{track} = mv^2/R$ if track pushes inward, giving $N = mv^2/R - mg = m(kd^2/m - 2gR)/R - mg = kd^2/(mR) - 2mg - mg = kd^2/(mR) - 3mg$. But this assumes full circle geometry. For quarter pipe from ground to height $R$, block is at height $R$ moving horizontally. If the track is on the inside of the curve (like a half-pipe), normal force from track points down (toward center below). No, the normal force from the track at the top of a quarter-pipe pushes the block inward (toward center). At height $R$, if moving horizontally and center is below, then centripetal acceleration points down. The track pushes down (if on the inside) — but physically the track is below the block. Let me reconsider: on a quarter-circle track going from horizontal ground curving up to vertical height $R$, at the very top the velocity is horizontal and the radius of curvature center is at ground level directly below. The block is on the inside of the curve. Normal force from track points upward (away from center). Weight points toward center (down). So: $mg - N = mv^2/R$ gives $N = mg - mv^2/R$. If $v$ is large, $N$ becomes negative meaning the block leaves the track.1

FRQ 2: Rotating Rod with Attached Mass (15 points)

Part (a) — Moment of inertia (2 points)

CriteriaPoints
Rod about end: $I_{rod} = ML^2/3$1
Point mass at distance $L$: $I_{ball} = ML^2$. Total: $I = ML^2/3 + ML^2 = 4ML^2/3$1

Part (b) — Angular acceleration at release (4 points)

CriteriaPoints
Identify torques about pivot: $\tau_{rod} = Mg(L/2)$, $\tau_{ball} = MgL$1
Total torque: $\tau = MgL/2 + MgL = 3MgL/2$1
Apply $\tau = I\alpha$: $3MgL/2 = (4ML^2/3)\alpha$1
Solve: $\alpha = \frac{9g}{8L}$1

Part (c) — Angular velocity when vertical (4 points)

CriteriaPoints
Use energy conservation (not constant $\alpha$)1
PE lost: rod CM falls $L/2$, ball falls $L$. $\Delta PE = Mg(L/2) + MgL = 3MgL/2$1
KE gained: $\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}(4ML^2/3)\omega^2$1
Solve: $3MgL/2 = 2ML^2\omega^2/3 \Rightarrow \omega = \sqrt{9g/(8L)} = \frac{3}{2}\sqrt{g/(2L)}$1

Part (d) — Pivot force when vertical (5 points)

CriteriaPoints
Recognize centripetal acceleration of CM: $a_c = \omega^2 \cdot r_{cm}$, where $r_{cm} = (M \cdot L/2 + M \cdot L)/(2M) = 3L/4$1
Radial (upward) direction: $F_{pivot} - 2Mg = (2M)\omega^2(3L/4)$1
Substituting $\omega^2 = 9g/(8L)$: centripetal term $= 2M \cdot \frac{9g}{8L} \cdot \frac{3L}{4} = \frac{27Mg}{16}$1
Also consider tangential: $\alpha$ at vertical position. $\tau = 0$ (if vertical means forces act through pivot — no, gravity still acts). Actually at vertical, torque about pivot = $Mg(0) + Mg(0) = 0$ only if the rod hangs straight down. Yes, when vertical (hanging down), all weight acts along the radial direction. So $\alpha = 0$ at that instant.1
Final: $F_{pivot} = 2Mg + 27Mg/16 = 32Mg/16 + 27Mg/16 = 59Mg/16$, directed upward1

Note: Accept equivalent correct approaches. The key physics: at the vertical position, there is no tangential acceleration (torque is zero), but there is centripetal acceleration directed upward toward the pivot. The pivot must support both weight and provide centripetal force.

FRQ 3: Oscillating System & Experimental Design (15 points)

Part (a) — Effective spring constant and period (3 points)

CriteriaPoints
For parallel springs, both displaced by same $x$: $F_{net} = -kx - kx = -2kx$, so $k_{eff} = 2k$1
Period formula: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k_{eff}}$1
Final: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/(2k)} = \pi\sqrt{2m/k}$1

Part (b) — Velocity as function of position (3 points)

CriteriaPoints
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 = \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}x^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$1
Solve for $v$: $v = \sqrt{\frac{k_{eff}}{m}(A^2-x^2)}$1
Substitute $k_{eff} = 2k$: $v = \sqrt{\frac{2k}{m}(A^2-x^2)}$1

Part (c) — Amplitude after one cycle with friction (4 points)

CriteriaPoints
Recognize friction does negative work each half-cycle: $W_f = -\mu_k mg \cdot d$ where $d$ is distance traveled1
In one full cycle, block travels total distance $4A'_{avg} \approx 4A$ (approximation for light damping)1
Energy equation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 - \mu_k mg(4A)$1
Solve: $A_f = \sqrt{A^2 - \frac{4\mu_k mg A}{k_{eff}}}$ or linearized: $A_f \approx A - \frac{2\mu_k mg}{k}$ (for small friction)1

Part (d) — Experimental design (5 points)

CriteriaPoints
Procedure: Attach known mass to spring(s), displace and release, measure period of oscillation using stopwatch (time multiple oscillations and divide)1
Measurements: Mass $m$ (known), time for $N$ oscillations $\Rightarrow$ period $T = t/N$1
Multiple trials: Repeat for several different masses to reduce uncertainty1
Graph: Plot $T^2$ vs. $m$. Since $T^2 = 4\pi^2 m/k_{eff}$, the slope = $4\pi^2/k_{eff}$, giving $k_{eff} = 4\pi^2/\text{slope}$, then $k = k_{eff}/2$1
Error reduction: Time many oscillations (e.g., 20) and divide; use multiple masses; ensure small amplitude for SHM validity; use ruler to ensure consistent release amplitude1

Alternative valid approach: Static method — hang masses vertically from spring, measure extension with ruler, plot $F=mg$ vs $x$. Slope = $k$. Full credit for any valid, well-described method.

Advanced Exam Strategies

Multiple Choice Strategies

  • Dimensional analysis: If unsure, eliminate answers with wrong units. This alone can eliminate 2-3 choices.
  • Limiting cases: Test extreme values (mass → 0, angle → 0 or 90°). If an answer gives absurd results in a limit, it's wrong.
  • Order of magnitude: Estimate roughly before calculating precisely. If your answer is 10x off from all choices, you made an error.
  • Time management: Spend ~75 seconds per question. If stuck after 2 minutes, flag it and move on. Return to flagged questions at the end.
  • Sign conventions: Always define positive direction before starting. Many wrong answers come from sign errors.

Free Response Strategies

  • Show ALL work: Partial credit is generous. Write every equation, even if obvious. A correct equation with wrong algebra still earns points.
  • Start with what you know: Even if you can't complete a part, write the relevant equation. You can earn 1-2 points just for setup.
  • Use results from previous parts: If part (a) gives you a velocity you couldn't find, use a symbol and proceed. You won't lose points for carrying forward a wrong number if the method is correct.
  • Draw diagrams: Free-body diagrams, energy bar charts, before/after sketches. These earn points and prevent errors.
  • Check units and limits: After deriving an expression, verify units are correct and that limiting cases make physical sense. Graders look for this.
  • 15 minutes per FRQ: Budget time strictly. A partial answer on all three is better than a perfect answer on one.

Key Formulas to Have at Fingertips

  • Rolling without slipping: $v_{cm} = R\omega$, $a_{cm} = R\alpha$, $a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1+I/(mR^2)}$
  • Physical pendulum: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{I/(mgd)}$
  • Parallel axis theorem: $I = I_{cm} + Md^2$
  • Reduced mass: $\mu = m_1m_2/(m_1+m_2)$
  • Rocket equation: $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/M_f)$
  • Elastic collision: $v_1' = \frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1 + \frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_2$
  • Escape velocity: $v_e = \sqrt{2GM/R} = v_{circular}\sqrt{2}$
  • SHM energy: $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2$; speed at $x$: $v = \omega\sqrt{A^2-x^2}$

The Confidence Rule

After each practice question, rate yourself:

  • "Confident" but WRONG = Most dangerous. You don't know what you don't know. Mark these for deep review.
  • "Guessing" but RIGHT = You have more intuition than you think. Formalize this knowledge so you can reproduce it reliably.
  • "Confident" and RIGHT = Great! Move on. Don't over-study things you already know.
  • "Guessing" and WRONG = Expected. This is a knowledge gap to fill.
