Kinematics (Q1-6)

Question 1 Kinematics
Answer: (A) 0 m
Key concept: Displacement = integral of velocity (calculus required).

Step 1: Write the displacement integral:
$\Delta x = \int_0^6 v(t)\,dt = \int_0^6 (4t - t^2)\,dt$

Step 2: Evaluate the antiderivative:
$= \left[2t^2 - \frac{t^3}{3}\right]_0^6$

Step 3: Plug in bounds:
$= \left(2(36) - \frac{216}{3}\right) - (0) = 72 - 72 = 0$ m

Why it's zero: The velocity $v = 4t - t^2 = t(4-t)$ is positive from $t=0$ to $t=4$ and negative from $t=4$ to $t=6$. The particle moves forward then backward, returning to its starting position.
Common mistake: Getting 12 m by computing total distance (adding absolute values of each interval) instead of net displacement. Or getting 36 by plugging $t=6$ into $v(t)$ without integrating — velocity is NOT displacement!

How to explain: "Displacement asks WHERE you ended up. Distance asks HOW FAR you walked. If you walk 5 m forward and 5 m back, displacement is 0 but distance is 10 m. The integral of velocity gives displacement."
Question 2Kinematics
Answer: (C) $3H/4$
Key concept: Energy conservation — KE converts to PE. Speed and height are related through $v^2$, not $v$.

Step 1: Express $H$ in terms of $v_0$. At max height, all KE becomes PE:
$\frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = mgH \Rightarrow H = \frac{v_0^2}{2g}$

Step 2: Use kinematics at height $h$ where speed is $v_0/2$:
$v^2 = v_0^2 - 2gh \Rightarrow \left(\frac{v_0}{2}\right)^2 = v_0^2 - 2gh$

Step 3: Solve for $h$:
$\frac{v_0^2}{4} = v_0^2 - 2gh \Rightarrow 2gh = \frac{3v_0^2}{4} \Rightarrow h = \frac{3v_0^2}{8g} = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{v_0^2}{2g} = \frac{3H}{4}$
Common mistake: Choosing $H/2$ by thinking "half the speed = half the height." This is wrong because KE depends on $v^2$. Half the speed means $1/4$ of the original KE remains, so $3/4$ has converted to PE, meaning the ball is at $3/4$ of the maximum height.

How to explain: "Energy goes as speed SQUARED. So if speed drops to half, kinetic energy drops to one-quarter. That means three-quarters of the energy has become potential energy, so you're at three-quarters of the max height."
Question 3Kinematics
Answer: (C) $g$, directed downward
Key concept: In projectile motion, acceleration is ALWAYS $g$ downward, at every point in the trajectory.

The reasoning: The only force acting on the projectile (ignoring air resistance) is gravity: $\vec{F} = -mg\hat{j}$. By Newton's second law, $\vec{a} = \vec{F}/m = -g\hat{j}$. This is constant — it does not depend on the position or velocity of the projectile.

At the highest point, $v_y = 0$ but $v_x = v_0\cos\theta \neq 0$. The velocity is purely horizontal, but the acceleration is still $g$ downward.
Common mistake: Choosing (A) "$0$" — confusing zero vertical velocity with zero acceleration. Velocity and acceleration are different quantities! A ball at the top of its arc is momentarily not moving vertically, but gravity is still pulling it down (that's why it comes back down).

How to explain: "If the acceleration were zero at the top, the ball would just keep going horizontally forever. The fact that it curves back down proves the acceleration is NOT zero — gravity never takes a break."
Question 4Kinematics
Answer: (C) $t = 2$ s and $t = 4$ s
Key concept: "At rest" means $v = 0$. Velocity is the derivative of position.

Step 1: Differentiate position to get velocity:
$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2 - 18t + 24$

Step 2: Set $v = 0$ and factor:
$3t^2 - 18t + 24 = 0 \Rightarrow t^2 - 6t + 8 = 0 \Rightarrow (t-2)(t-4) = 0$

Step 3: Solutions: $t = 2$ s and $t = 4$ s
Common mistake: Setting $x(t) = 0$ instead of $v(t) = 0$. "At rest" means zero VELOCITY, not zero position. Position = 0 means the particle is at the origin; velocity = 0 means it has stopped momentarily.

How to explain: "Think of a ball thrown straight up — at the very top, it's at rest (v=0) for an instant, but it's NOT at position zero. 'At rest' always means take the derivative and set it to zero."
Question 5Kinematics
Answer: (C) They hit the ground at the same time
Key concept: Independence of horizontal and vertical motion in projectile motion.

The reasoning: Both objects start with the same vertical velocity ($v_{y,0} = 0$) from the same height. The vertical equation of motion is the same for both:
$y = h - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
Setting $y = 0$: $t = \sqrt{2h/g}$ — identical for both objects.

Object B's horizontal velocity ($v$) has no effect on the vertical motion because horizontal and vertical motions are independent (no air resistance).
Common mistake: Thinking that the horizontal velocity "uses up" some of the object's ability to fall, or that a longer path means more time. Horizontal and vertical are completely decoupled.

How to explain: "Imagine two balls side by side — one you drop, one you flick sideways. Gravity pulls them both down equally. The flicked one goes farther horizontally but falls at the exact same rate. Try it with two coins off a table edge!"
Question 6Kinematics
Answer: (C) 9 m/s
Key concept: With non-constant acceleration, must integrate to find velocity (calculus trap).

Step 1: Find when $v$ is maximum. Velocity increases while $a > 0$ and decreases when $a < 0$. So max $v$ occurs when $a = 0$:
$6 - 2t = 0 \Rightarrow t = 3$ s

Step 2: Integrate acceleration to find velocity (starting from rest, $v_0 = 0$):
$v(3) = \int_0^3 (6 - 2t)\,dt = \left[6t - t^2\right]_0^3 = 18 - 9 = 9$ m/s
Common mistake: Using $v = at$ with $a = 6$ gives $v = 18$ m/s — wrong because acceleration is NOT constant. Or using the average $a_{avg} = 3$ m/s$^2$ with $v = a_{avg} \times t = 9$, which accidentally gives the right answer but for the wrong reason. Must integrate.

How to explain: "When acceleration changes with time, you can't use $v = v_0 + at$. That formula only works for constant acceleration. With variable acceleration, you MUST integrate: $v = \int a\,dt$. This is why Physics C requires calculus."

Newton's Laws (Q7-12)

Question 7Newton's Laws
Answer: (C) $mg\tan\theta$
Key concept: A horizontal force on an incline must be decomposed along and perpendicular to the incline surface.

Step 1: Draw the free-body diagram. Three forces act on the block: weight $mg$ (down), normal force $N$ (perpendicular to incline), and horizontal force $F$.

Step 2: Choose axes along and perpendicular to incline. Decompose $F$:
• Along incline (up): $F\cos\theta$
• Into incline: $F\sin\theta$

Step 3: Equilibrium along incline:
$F\cos\theta = mg\sin\theta \Rightarrow F = mg\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = mg\tan\theta$
Common mistake: Choosing $mg\sin\theta$ — that's the force needed if applied ALONG the incline. A horizontal force has a different component along the incline ($F\cos\theta$), so the required $F$ is larger.

How to explain: "The force is horizontal, but the incline is tilted. Only the component of $F$ that is parallel to the incline surface does the job of preventing sliding. Since that component is $F\cos\theta$, you need $F = mg\sin\theta/\cos\theta = mg\tan\theta$."
Question 8Newton's Laws
Answer: (D) 60 N
Key concept: Two-body problem — the friction between blocks must provide enough force to accelerate the top block along with the bottom block.

Step 1: What keeps the top block from sliding? Static friction. Max friction = $\mu_s m_{top} g = 0.4 \times 5 \times 10 = 20$ N

Step 2: This friction is the ONLY horizontal force on the top block, so it determines its max acceleration:
$a_{max} = \frac{f_{s,max}}{m_{top}} = \frac{20}{5} = 4$ m/s$^2$

Step 3: Both blocks must accelerate together at this rate. $F$ accelerates the entire system:
$F = (m_{top} + m_{bottom}) \times a_{max} = 15 \times 4 = 60$ N
Common mistake: Getting 20 N by thinking "the friction force is 20 N so $F = 20$ N." But $F$ must accelerate BOTH blocks (15 kg total), not just overcome friction. The 20 N friction only accelerates the 5 kg block.

How to explain: "Think of it this way: friction is what drags the top block along. The maximum friction (20 N) can accelerate the 5 kg block at 4 m/s$^2$. Now, the whole 15 kg system must accelerate at that same rate, so $F = 15 \times 4 = 60$ N."
Question 9Newton's Laws
Answer: (D) $5Mg/4$
Key concept: Newton's second law for an elevator. The tension must support the weight AND provide the upward acceleration.

Step 1: Draw FBD: tension $T$ (up), weight $Mg$ (down).

Step 2: Apply Newton's second law (taking up as positive):
$T - Mg = Ma$

Step 3: Solve with $a = g/4$:
$T = M(g + a) = M(g + g/4) = \frac{5Mg}{4}$
Common mistake: Getting $3Mg/4$ by writing $T = M(g - a)$. This sign error gives the tension for an elevator accelerating DOWNWARD. Quick check: if accelerating upward, you should feel heavier, so $T > Mg$. Since $5Mg/4 > Mg$, that's correct.

How to explain: "Stand on a scale in an elevator going up — you feel heavier. The cable has to pull harder than your weight to accelerate you upward. $T = M(g + a)$ always. If going down, $T = M(g - a)$."
Question 10Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $x = 0$ and $x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$
Key concept: Equilibrium occurs where the net force is zero. Factor carefully to find ALL solutions.

Step 1: Set $F = 0$: $-kx + bx^3 = 0$

Step 2: Factor out $x$: $x(-k + bx^2) = 0$

Step 3: Two cases:
• $x = 0$ (one solution)
• $bx^2 = k \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$ (two more solutions)

Stability note: $x = 0$ is stable (restoring force), while $x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$ are unstable (force pushes away).
Common mistake: Dividing both sides by $x$ early: $-k + bx^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm\sqrt{k/b}$ — this LOSES the $x = 0$ solution! Never divide by a variable that could be zero.

How to explain: "When you have $x \cdot (\text{something}) = 0$, you get TWO cases: either $x = 0$ OR the something = 0. If you divide by $x$, you're assuming $x \neq 0$ and you lose a solution. Always factor first, never divide by a variable."
Question 11Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g}$
Key concept: Conical pendulum — circular motion in the horizontal plane with gravity balanced by the vertical component of tension.

Step 1: The bob moves in a circle of radius $r = L\sin\theta$.

Step 2: Vertical equilibrium: $T\cos\theta = mg$
Step 3: Horizontal (centripetal): $T\sin\theta = m\omega^2 r = m\omega^2 L\sin\theta$

Step 4: Divide Step 3 by Step 2:
$\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{\omega^2 L\sin\theta}{g} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{\cos\theta} = \frac{\omega^2 L}{g}$
$\omega^2 = \frac{g}{L\cos\theta} \Rightarrow T_{period} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L\cos\theta}{g}}$
Common mistake: Using $2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ (simple pendulum formula). The conical pendulum is NOT the same as a simple pendulum — it moves in a circle, not back and forth. The $\cos\theta$ factor matters.

How to explain: "A conical pendulum swings around in a circle like a tetherball. The vertical component of tension holds up the weight, while the horizontal component provides centripetal force. It's a circular motion problem, not a simple pendulum problem."
Question 12Newton's Laws
Answer: (B) $g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$
Key concept: Block on a rough incline — decompose forces along and perpendicular to the surface.

Step 1: Forces along incline (positive = down the slope):
• Gravity component down: $mg\sin\theta$
• Friction up (opposes motion): $f_k = \mu_k N$

Step 2: Perpendicular to incline: $N = mg\cos\theta$

Step 3: Newton's second law along incline:
$ma = mg\sin\theta - \mu_k mg\cos\theta$
$a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$
Common mistake: Getting choice (A) $g(\sin\theta + \mu_k\cos\theta)$ by adding friction instead of subtracting. Friction OPPOSES motion — if the block slides down, friction acts UP the incline, so subtract it.

How to explain: "Always ask: which way is the block moving? Then friction points the opposite way. Block slides down → friction points up the incline → subtract friction from the gravity component."

Work, Energy & Power (Q13-18)

Question 13Work & Energy
Answer: (B) 18 J
Key concept: Work by a variable force = integral of $F\,dx$ (cannot use $W = Fd$).

Step 1: Set up the integral: $W = \int_1^3 (3x^2 - 2x)\,dx$

Step 2: Find the antiderivative: $= \left[x^3 - x^2\right]_1^3$

Step 3: Evaluate: $= (27 - 9) - (1 - 1) = 18 - 0 = 18$ J
Common mistake: Using $W = F \times d = F(3) \times 2 = 42$ J — this is wrong because the force varies with position. $W = Fd$ only works for constant forces. Variable forces require integration.

How to explain: "When force changes as you move, you can't just multiply. Think of it like computing area under a curve — you need calculus. $W = \int F\,dx$ is the area under the force-position graph."
Question 14Work & Energy
Answer: (C) $\sqrt{2g(h - \mu_k d)}$
Key concept: Work-energy theorem with friction. Friction does negative work that removes energy from the system.

Step 1: Initial energy (top of track): $E_i = mgh$ (all PE, starts from rest)

Step 2: Energy lost to friction on horizontal section: $W_{friction} = \mu_k mg \cdot d$

Step 3: Final KE after rough section: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgh - \mu_k mgd$

Step 4: Solve: $v = \sqrt{2g(h - \mu_k d)}$

Note: This equals $\sqrt{2gh - 2\mu_k gd}$ (choice B), which is algebraically identical.
Common mistake: Choice (A) forgets the factor of 2 from $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Choice (D) subtracts $\mu_k gd$ outside the square root, which is dimensionally wrong (you'd be subtracting m/s from m$^2$/s$^2$).

How to explain: "Friction steals energy. Start with $mgh$ of PE, subtract $\mu_k mgd$ of friction loss, what's left becomes KE. Then solve $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = $ leftover energy for $v$."
Question 15Work & Energy
Answer: (A) $x = 0$ only
Key concept: Stable equilibrium = local MINIMUM of potential energy. Must check both $F = 0$ AND stability via second derivative.

Step 1: Find equilibrium points ($F = 0$):
$F = -\frac{dU}{dx} = -(8x - 4x^3) = 0 \Rightarrow 4x(2 - x^2) = 0$
Solutions: $x = 0$ and $x = \pm\sqrt{2}$

Step 2: Check stability using $\frac{d^2U}{dx^2}$:
$\frac{d^2U}{dx^2} = 8 - 12x^2$
• At $x = 0$: $\frac{d^2U}{dx^2} = 8 > 0$ → concave up → local minimum → STABLE
• At $x = \pm\sqrt{2}$: $\frac{d^2U}{dx^2} = 8 - 24 = -16 < 0$ → concave down → local maximum → UNSTABLE

Only $x = 0$ is stable equilibrium.
Common mistake: Choosing (C) — finding all three equilibrium points but not checking which are stable. The question specifically asks for STABLE equilibrium. A ball at the top of a hill ($U$ maximum) is in equilibrium but will roll away if nudged — that's unstable.

How to explain: "Think of a bowl vs a hilltop. A ball at the bottom of a bowl (U minimum) returns to the center if pushed — that's stable. A ball on a hilltop (U maximum) rolls away — that's unstable. The second derivative tells you which one you have: positive = bowl = stable."
Question 16Work & Energy
Answer: (C) $kd^2/(2mg)$
Key concept: Spring PE converts entirely to gravitational PE at maximum height.

Step 1: Spring PE when compressed: $U_{spring} = \frac{1}{2}kd^2$

Step 2: At max height, all energy is gravitational PE: $U_{grav} = mgh$

Step 3: Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}kd^2 = mgh \Rightarrow h = \frac{kd^2}{2mg}$
Common mistake: Forgetting the $\frac{1}{2}$ in spring PE gives $h = kd^2/(mg)$ (choice B — off by a factor of 2). Or confusing $kd$ with $kd^2$ (choice A) — spring energy depends on displacement SQUARED.

How to explain: "The spring formula is $\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ — two things to remember: the $\frac{1}{2}$ AND the $x$ is squared. Both are common exam traps."
Question 17Work & Energy
Answer: (D) 6000 W
Key concept: Power = force times velocity for constant speed. $P = Fv$.

Step 1: At constant speed, the net force is zero. The motor force equals gravity: $F = mg$

Step 2: Power: $P = Fv = mgv = 200 \times 10 \times 3 = 6000$ W

(Using $g = 10$ m/s$^2$ as standard for AP Physics MC)
Common mistake: Getting 600 W by forgetting to multiply by $g$ (using $P = mv$ instead of $P = mgv$). Force is not mass — force is mass times $g$ when lifting against gravity.

How to explain: "At constant speed, the motor has to push with exactly the weight of the crate ($mg$). Power is how fast you do work: $P = Fv$. So $P = mg \times v$."
Question 18Work & Energy
Answer: (B) $\sqrt{20}$ m/s
Key concept: Work = area under force-displacement graph. Then use work-energy theorem.

Step 1: The graph is a triangle (F = 10 N at x = 0, linearly to F = 0 at x = 4 m):
$W = \text{area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}(4)(10) = 20$ J

Step 2: Work-energy theorem (starts from rest):
$W = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - 0$
$20 = \frac{1}{2}(2)v^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 20 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}$ m/s
Common mistake: Using $W = F \times d = 10 \times 4 = 40$ J (treating it as a rectangle instead of a triangle). The force is NOT constant — it decreases linearly. Must read the graph shape and compute the correct area.

How to explain: "The work done by a variable force is the AREA under the F vs. x graph. A triangle has area = $\frac{1}{2}$ base $\times$ height. If you use the full rectangle, you're pretending the force never decreased — that gives double the correct work."

Momentum & Collisions (Q19-24)

Question 19 Momentum
Answer: (B) 1 m/s in the opposite direction
Elastic collision formulas:
Key concept: In elastic collisions, use the derived formula (saves time over solving two simultaneous equations).

$v_1' = \frac{m_1 - m_2}{m_1 + m_2}v_1 = \frac{2-3}{5}(5) = \frac{-1}{5}(5) = -1$ m/s

Negative means opposite direction. Speed = 1 m/s in the opposite direction.
Common mistake: Using only momentum conservation gives $v_f = (2 \times 5)/(2+3) = 2$ m/s — but that's the perfectly INELASTIC result (objects stick together). Elastic collisions conserve both momentum AND kinetic energy.

How to explain: "For elastic collisions, memorize this formula: $v_1' = \frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1$. Lighter ball bounces back (negative), heavier ball barely moves. If masses are equal, the first ball stops completely."
Question 20 Momentum
Answer: (B) $v\sqrt{2}/3$
Conservation of momentum (initially at rest, total $p = 0$):
$\vec{p}_1 + \vec{p}_2 + \vec{p}_3 = 0$
Two pieces perpendicular, each momentum $= mv$:
$|\vec{p}_1 + \vec{p}_2| = \sqrt{(mv)^2 + (mv)^2} = mv\sqrt{2}$
$p_3 = 3m \cdot v_3 = mv\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow v_3 = v\sqrt{2}/3$
Common mistake: Adding momenta algebraically ($2mv$) instead of vectorially. Since the pieces are perpendicular, use Pythagorean theorem: $\sqrt{(mv)^2 + (mv)^2} = mv\sqrt{2}$, NOT $mv + mv = 2mv$.

How to explain: "Momentum is a vector! When two momenta are perpendicular, you can't just add them — use the Pythagorean theorem. It's like walking 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north: you're 5 blocks away, not 7."
Question 21 Momentum
Answer: (C) $2mv\cos\theta$
The component of velocity parallel to the wall is unchanged. The component perpendicular to the wall reverses.
$\Delta p = 2m(v\cos\theta)$ (perpendicular component reverses)
Impulse $= |\Delta \vec{p}| = 2mv\cos\theta$
Common mistake: Getting $2mv$ by ignoring the angle (that's only true for head-on collision, $\theta = 0$). Or $2mv\sin\theta$ by confusing which component reverses — since $\theta$ is from the NORMAL, the perpendicular component is $v\cos\theta$ (cos, not sin).

How to explain: "Only the component hitting the wall head-on reverses. The component sliding along the wall doesn't change. Since the angle is from the normal (perpendicular to wall), the head-on component is $v\cos\theta$. It reverses: change = $2mv\cos\theta$."
Question 22 Momentum
Answer: (B) $v_e \ln 2$
Tsiolkovsky rocket equation: $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/M_f)$
With $M_f = M_0/2$: $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/(M_0/2)) = v_e \ln 2$
Common mistake: Writing $v_e \times (M_0 - M_f)/M_0 = v_e/2$ — this linear approximation is wrong because as the rocket gets lighter, each bit of expelled fuel gives MORE acceleration. The logarithm captures this cumulative effect.

How to explain: "The rocket equation $\Delta v = v_e \ln(M_0/M_f)$ is one of the few formulas worth memorizing. The $\ln$ appears because the thrust is constant but the mass keeps decreasing, so the acceleration increases over time. You integrate $F/m(t)$ and that integral gives you a logarithm."
Question 23 Momentum
Answer: (C) $3R/8$
This is a standard result. Using integration with disk elements:
$\bar{z} = \frac{\int_0^R z \cdot \pi(R^2-z^2)\rho\,dz}{\frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 \rho} = \frac{3}{8}R$
Common mistake: Confusing with hemisphere shell ($R/2$) or half-sphere volume centroid formulas. This is one to memorize for the exam.
Question 24 Momentum
Answer: (C) 6 m/s
Perfectly inelastic: $m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1+m_2)v_f$
$1(10) + 2(4) = 3v_f \Rightarrow v_f = 18/3 = 6$ m/s
Common mistake: Averaging speeds: $(10+4)/2 = 7$ m/s. This only works if masses are equal. With unequal masses, you must weight by mass: $v_f = (m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2)/(m_1 + m_2)$.

How to explain: "Perfectly inelastic means they stick together. Just conserve momentum: total momentum before = total momentum after. $p_{before} = 1(10) + 2(4) = 18$. Total mass after = 3 kg. So $v = 18/3 = 6$ m/s."

Rotation (Q25-30)

Question 25 Rotation
Answer: (C) $(2/3)g\sin\theta$
Key concept: Rolling without slipping — energy goes into BOTH translation and rotation, so the acceleration is reduced.

The master formula: $a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + I/(MR^2)}$

For a solid disk: $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$, so $I/(MR^2) = 1/2$
$a = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + 1/2} = \frac{g\sin\theta}{3/2} = \frac{2}{3}g\sin\theta$
Common mistake: Using $g\sin\theta$ (that's for sliding without friction — no rotation). Or using the wrong moment of inertia: disk = $\frac{1}{2}MR^2$, sphere = $\frac{2}{5}MR^2$, shell = $\frac{2}{3}MR^2$, hoop = $MR^2$.

How to explain: "When something rolls, gravity has to spin it AND slide it. That 'tax' for spinning slows it down. The more rotational inertia, the more tax, the slower it rolls. Memorize: $a = g\sin\theta / (1 + I/(MR^2))$ — it works for any shape."
Question 26 Rotation
Answer: (C) $\sqrt{3g/L}$
Energy conservation: PE lost = KE gained
CM falls $L/2$: $Mg(L/2) = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
For rod about end: $I = ML^2/3$
$Mg(L/2) = \frac{1}{2}(ML^2/3)\omega^2$
$\omega = \sqrt{3g/L}$
Common mistake: Using $I = ML^2/12$ (center pivot instead of end). Or having CM fall by $L$ instead of $L/2$. The pivot is at one end, so CM is at $L/2$ from pivot and falls that distance.
Question 27 Rotation
Answer: (A) The solid sphere arrives first and has greater speed
$a = g\sin\theta/(1 + I/(MR^2))$
Solid sphere: $I = 2MR^2/5$, factor = $1 + 2/5 = 7/5$, $a = 5g\sin\theta/7$
Hollow shell: $I = 2MR^2/3$, factor = $1 + 2/3 = 5/3$, $a = 3g\sin\theta/5$
Since $5/7 > 3/5$, solid sphere has greater acceleration, arrives first, and has greater speed.
Common mistake: Thinking same mass/radius means same behavior. The distribution of mass matters — more mass concentrated far from axis (shell) means more rotational inertia and slower rolling.
Question 28 Rotation
Answer: (B) Increases by a factor of 3
Angular momentum conserved: $L = I\omega_0 = (I/3)\omega_f \Rightarrow \omega_f = 3\omega_0$
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_0^2$
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(I/3)(3\omega_0)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(I/3)(9\omega_0^2) = \frac{3}{2}I\omega_0^2 = 3 \times KE_i$
Common mistake: Thinking energy is conserved (choice A) — the skater's muscles do internal work! Or getting factor of 9 by computing $\omega_f^2/\omega_0^2 = 9$ but forgetting that $I$ also changed (by factor $1/3$), so the net KE change is $9 \times 1/3 = 3$.

How to explain: "Angular momentum is conserved (no external torque), but energy is NOT — the skater does work with her muscles. $L = I\omega$ stays constant, so if $I$ drops by 3, $\omega$ triples. KE = $\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = L^2/(2I)$. Since $I$ drops by 3, KE increases by 3."
Question 29 Rotation
Answer: (C) $1/2$
Angular momentum conserved: $I\omega = 2I\omega_f \Rightarrow \omega_f = \omega/2$
$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(2I)(\omega/2)^2 = \frac{1}{4}I\omega^2$
Fraction lost $= (KE_i - KE_f)/KE_i = 1 - 1/2 = 1/2$
Common mistake: Thinking no energy is lost because angular momentum is conserved. Conservation of $L$ does NOT mean conservation of $KE$! The friction between the disks converts kinetic energy to heat.

How to explain: "This is like an inelastic collision but for rotation. Angular momentum is conserved (like linear momentum in collisions), but kinetic energy is lost to friction between the disks. $KE_f/KE_i = I/(2I) \times (\omega/2)^2/(\omega^2) = 1/2$. So half the energy is lost."
Question 30 Rotation
Answer: (A) $\tau_0 T/I$
$\omega = \frac{1}{I}\int_0^\infty \tau_0 e^{-t/T}\,dt = \frac{\tau_0}{I}[-T e^{-t/T}]_0^\infty = \frac{\tau_0}{I}(0-(-T)) = \frac{\tau_0 T}{I}$
Common mistake: Forgetting the factor of $T$ from the chain rule. $\int e^{-t/T}dt = -Te^{-t/T}$, NOT $-e^{-t/T}$. The inner derivative $(-1/T)$ creates a factor of $T$ in the result.

How to explain: "This is a pure calculus question in a physics disguise. $\omega = \frac{1}{I}\int_0^\infty \tau_0 e^{-t/T}dt$. To integrate $e^{-t/T}$, use substitution: let $u = -t/T$, $du = -dt/T$, so $dt = -T\,du$. The integral becomes $-T[e^{-t/T}]_0^\infty = -T(0-1) = T$. Final answer: $\tau_0 T/I$."

Oscillations & Gravitation (Q31-35)

Question 31 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (B) $\sqrt{3kA^2/(4m)}$
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
At $x = A/2$: $\frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}k(A/2)^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
$kA^2 - kA^2/4 = mv^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = \frac{3kA^2}{4m}$
$v = \sqrt{3kA^2/(4m)} = \frac{A}{2}\sqrt{3k/m}$
Common mistake: Using $v = \omega A$ everywhere — that's the MAX speed (at $x = 0$ only). At other positions, use $v = \omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}$.

How to explain: "In SHM, total energy = $\frac{1}{2}kA^2$ is shared between PE ($\frac{1}{2}kx^2$) and KE ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$). At $x = A/2$: PE uses $1/4$ of total energy (since $(A/2)^2 = A^2/4$), leaving $3/4$ for KE. So $v^2 = \frac{3kA^2}{4m}$."
Question 32 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (D) $T\sqrt{2}$
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. On new planet: $g' = GM'/R'^2 = G(2M)/(2R)^2 = 2GM/(4R^2) = g/2$
$T' = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g'} = 2\pi\sqrt{2L/g} = T\sqrt{2}$
Common mistake: Thinking "double mass = double gravity" — this ignores that radius is also doubled and appears SQUARED in the denominator. $g = GM/R^2$, so doubling both $M$ and $R$: $g' = G(2M)/(2R)^2 = 2GM/(4R^2) = g/2$. Gravity is actually HALVED!

How to explain: "Surface gravity depends on $M/R^2$. If you double both M and R: the numerator doubles but the denominator quadruples (since $R$ is squared). Net effect: gravity is halved. With half the gravity, the pendulum swings slower by factor $\sqrt{2}$."
Question 33 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (C) Escape Earth's gravitational field
Circular orbit speed: $v_c = \sqrt{GM/r}$. Escape speed: $v_e = \sqrt{2GM/r} = v_c\sqrt{2}$.
If speed is increased by factor $\sqrt{2}$: new speed $= v_c\sqrt{2} = v_e$.
The satellite reaches exactly escape velocity and will escape to infinity.
Common mistake: Thinking a larger speed means a bigger circular orbit. Increasing speed by $\sqrt{2}$ is the EXACT factor needed to escape — this is a key relationship to know.

How to explain: "There's a beautiful relationship: escape speed = orbital speed $\times \sqrt{2}$. So if you multiply an orbiting satellite's speed by $\sqrt{2}$, it has EXACTLY enough energy to escape to infinity. This comes from $v_{escape} = \sqrt{2GM/r}$ vs $v_{orbit} = \sqrt{GM/r}$."
Question 34 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (C) $2\pi\sqrt{2L/(3g)}$
Physical pendulum: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{I/(mgd)}$
Rod pivoted at end: $I = ML^2/3$, distance to CM: $d = L/2$
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{ML^2/3}{Mg(L/2)}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{2L}{3g}}$
Common mistake: Using simple pendulum formula $2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ (treating rod as point mass). Or using $d = L$ instead of $d = L/2$ for the CM distance.
Question 35 Oscillations & Gravitation
Answer: (B) $\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k(m_1+m_2)}{m_1 m_2}}$
Two-body oscillation uses reduced mass: $\mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$
$f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{k/\mu} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k(m_1+m_2)}{m_1 m_2}}$
Note: choices (B) and (C) are equivalent since $m_1m_2/(m_1+m_2) = \mu$.

CriteriaPoints

CriteriaPoints

FRQ 3: Oscillating System & Experimental Design (15 points)

Part (a) — Effective spring constant and period (3 points)

CriteriaPoints
For parallel springs, both displaced by same $x$: $F_{net} = -kx - kx = -2kx$, so $k_{eff} = 2k$1
Period formula: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k_{eff}}$1
Final: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/(2k)} = \pi\sqrt{2m/k}$1

Part (b) — Velocity as function of position (3 points)

CriteriaPoints
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 = \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}x^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$1
Solve for $v$: $v = \sqrt{\frac{k_{eff}}{m}(A^2-x^2)}$1
Substitute $k_{eff} = 2k$: $v = \sqrt{\frac{2k}{m}(A^2-x^2)}$1

Part (c) — Amplitude after one cycle with friction (4 points)

CriteriaPoints
Recognize friction does negative work each half-cycle: $W_f = -\mu_k mg \cdot d$ where $d$ is distance traveled1
In one full cycle, block travels total distance $4A'_{avg} \approx 4A$ (approximation for light damping)1
Energy equation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 - \mu_k mg(4A)$1
Solve: $A_f = \sqrt{A^2 - \frac{4\mu_k mg A}{k_{eff}}}$ or linearized: $A_f \approx A - \frac{2\mu_k mg}{k}$ (for small friction)1

Part (d) — Experimental design (5 points)

CriteriaPoints
Procedure: Attach known mass to spring(s), displace and release, measure period of oscillation using stopwatch (time multiple oscillations and divide)1
Measurements: Mass $m$ (known), time for $N$ oscillations $\Rightarrow$ period $T = t/N$1
Multiple trials: Repeat for several different masses to reduce uncertainty1
Graph: Plot $T^2$ vs. $m$. Since $T^2 = 4\pi^2 m/k_{eff}$, the slope = $4\pi^2/k_{eff}$, giving $k_{eff} = 4\pi^2/\text{slope}$, then $k = k_{eff}/2$1
Error reduction: Time many oscillations (e.g., 20) and divide; use multiple masses; ensure small amplitude for SHM validity; use ruler to ensure consistent release amplitude1

Advanced Exam Strategies

Multiple Choice Strategies

  • Dimensional analysis: If unsure, eliminate answers with wrong units. This alone can eliminate 2-3 choices.
  • Limiting cases: Test extreme values (mass → 0, angle → 0 or 90°). If an answer gives absurd results in a limit, it's wrong.
  • Order of magnitude: Estimate roughly before calculating precisely. If your answer is 10x off from all choices, you made an error.
  • Time management: Spend ~75 seconds per question. If stuck after 2 minutes, flag it and move on. Return to flagged questions at the end.
  • Sign conventions: Always define positive direction before starting. Many wrong answers come from sign errors.

Free Response Strategies

  • Show ALL work: Partial credit is generous. Write every equation, even if obvious. A correct equation with wrong algebra still earns points.
  • Start with what you know: Even if you can't complete a part, write the relevant equation. You can earn 1-2 points just for setup.
  • Use results from previous parts: If part (a) gives you a velocity you couldn't find, use a symbol and proceed. You won't lose points for carrying forward a wrong number if the method is correct.
  • Draw diagrams: Free-body diagrams, energy bar charts, before/after sketches. These earn points and prevent errors.
  • Check units and limits: After deriving an expression, verify units are correct and that limiting cases make physical sense. Graders look for this.
  • 15 minutes per FRQ: Budget time strictly. A partial answer on all three is better than a perfect answer on one.
