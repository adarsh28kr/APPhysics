MOMENTUM & IMPULSE REFRESHER

AP Physics C: Mechanics • 10 Topic Areas • Formulas, Intuition & AP Tips
The Big Idea
$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$   •   $\vec{F}_{net} = \dfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$
Momentum is "mass in motion." Force is the rate of change of momentum. If no external force acts, momentum is conserved.

1 Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Core idea: Impulse = change in momentum. A force applied over time changes an object's momentum.
$J = \Delta p = F \cdot \Delta t = mv_f - mv_i$
Intuition: A small force for a long time has the same impulse as a large force for a short time. That's why airbags work — they increase $\Delta t$ to reduce $F$.
Signs matter! If a ball bounces back, $\Delta p = m(-v_f) - m(v_i)$. Don't subtract magnitudes — use signs. A ball reversing direction has $\Delta p = m(v_f + v_i)$ in magnitude.
AP asks: "Find the impulse" or "Find the average force during a collision"

2 Impulse with Variable Force

When force changes with time, you can't just multiply $F \times \Delta t$. You must integrate.
$J = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} F(t)\,dt = \Delta p$
Graphically: Impulse = area under the $F$-$t$ graph. Triangles, rectangles, or curves — find the area.
AP asks: "Given $F(t) = 12t - 3t^2$, find the velocity at $t = 4$ s"

3 Conservation of Momentum

If no external force acts on the system:   $\vec{p}_i = \vec{p}_f$
When to use: Collisions, explosions, any interaction between objects where external forces are zero (or negligible during the event).
$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$
Key skill: Choose your SYSTEM wisely. Internal forces (the objects hitting each other) don't change total momentum. Only external forces (friction with ground, gravity) can — but during a brief collision, these are usually negligible.
Momentum is a vector! In 2D, conserve $p_x$ and $p_y$ separately.

4 Elastic Collisions (1D)

Definition: Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved.
$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$
$\frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2f}^2$
When target is at rest ($v_{2i} = 0$):
$v_{1f} = \dfrac{m_1 - m_2}{m_1 + m_2}\,v_{1i}$
$v_{2f} = \dfrac{2m_1}{m_1 + m_2}\,v_{1i}$
Special case: Equal masses ($m_1 = m_2$) → velocities SWAP. Moving ball stops, stationary ball takes its velocity.
Heavy hits light: heavy barely slows, light flies off fast. Light hits heavy: light bounces back, heavy barely moves.

5 Inelastic Collisions

Perfectly inelastic: Objects stick together. Maximum KE loss. Momentum still conserved.
$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2)\,v_f$
$v_f = \dfrac{m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i}}{m_1 + m_2}$
How to tell if a collision is elastic: Check if KE is conserved. Calculate $KE_{before}$ and $KE_{after}$. If equal = elastic. If $KE$ decreased = inelastic.
Energy is NOT conserved in inelastic collisions (KE is lost to heat/deformation). But momentum IS always conserved in all collisions.

6 2D Collisions

Key idea: Momentum is a vector. Conserve each component independently.
x: $m_1 v_{1x,i} + m_2 v_{2x,i} = m_1 v_{1x,f} + m_2 v_{2x,f}$
y: $m_1 v_{1y,i} + m_2 v_{2y,i} = m_1 v_{1y,f} + m_2 v_{2y,f}$
Equal-mass elastic 2D: The two balls always separate at $90°$ to each other. If ball 1 scatters at angle $\alpha$, ball 2 goes at $90° - \alpha$.
AP asks: "Ball A deflects at 30°. Find the y-component of Ball B's velocity."

7 Explosions

Reverse of perfectly inelastic. Object starts as one piece, breaks apart. Total momentum before = 0 (if at rest).
$0 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$  →  $m_1 v_1 = -m_2 v_2$
The pieces have equal and opposite momenta. The lighter piece moves faster. $KE$ increases (energy comes from internal source: spring, chemical, etc.).
AP asks: "A 5 kg object explodes into 2 kg and 3 kg pieces. If the 2 kg piece moves at 15 m/s east, find the speed of the 3 kg piece."

8 Center of Mass (Position)

$x_{cm} = \dfrac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + \cdots}{m_1 + m_2 + \cdots} = \dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{M}$
Intuition: The center of mass is the "balance point." It's closer to the heavier object. For equal masses, it's exactly in the middle.
For continuous objects: $x_{cm} = \frac{1}{M}\int x\,dm$
AP asks: "Three particles at positions $x_1, x_2, x_3$ with masses $m_1, m_2, m_3$. Find $x_{cm}$."

9 Center of Mass Motion

$v_{cm} = \dfrac{p_{total}}{M_{total}}$
$F_{ext} = M_{total}\,a_{cm}$
If $F_{ext} = 0$: the center of mass moves at constant velocity (or stays put), no matter what the pieces do.
Powerful shortcut: Two skaters push off each other on ice. CM doesn't move. An object explodes in midair. CM follows the same parabolic path as if no explosion happened.

10 Ballistic Pendulum

Classic 2-step problem: Bullet embeds in block (collision), then block swings up (pendulum).
Step 1 (Collision): $m v_0 = (m + M)v$
Use momentum (KE NOT conserved)
Step 2 (Swing): $\frac{1}{2}(m+M)v^2 = (m+M)gh$
Use energy (momentum NOT useful)
Never mix the two steps! Don't use energy through the collision (KE is lost). Don't use momentum through the swing (external force: gravity). Each step has its own conservation law.
AP asks: "A 10 g bullet embeds in a 2 kg block. The block swings up 0.05 m. Find the bullet's speed."

Which Conservation Law Do I Use?

Collision happening? → Use momentum ($p_i = p_f$)
Object moving up/down after collision? → Use energy ($KE \leftrightarrow PE$)
Is the collision elastic? → You can use BOTH momentum and energy simultaneously
Is it perfectly inelastic? → Momentum only (KE is lost)
Explosion? → Momentum (KE increases from internal energy)
