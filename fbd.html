The 6-Step Method — Follow This For EVERY Problem

Never skip a step. Do them in order. Every single time.

  1. Step 1 — Draw the Free Body Diagram. Isolate the object. Draw it as a simple box or dot. Show ALL forces acting ON it as arrows starting from the center. Forces to check: gravity ($mg$, always straight down), normal ($N$, ⊥ to surface), friction ($f$, along surface opposing motion), tension ($T$, along string pulling away), applied ($F$, as stated).
  2. Step 2 — Label each force. Write the name AND formula next to every arrow: $mg$, $N$, $f = \mu N$, $T$, $F$. If you can't label it, it probably shouldn't be there.
  3. Step 3 — Draw coordinate axes. Place the origin at the center of the object. Choose $x$-axis parallel to the surface the object moves on (or along the direction of motion). $y$-axis perpendicular to surface. On inclines: TILT your axes to match the slope. This is the key — once axes are tilted, the normal force is purely in $y$ and only gravity needs to be decomposed.
  4. Step 4 — Find the net force. Decompose any forces not aligned with your axes into $x$ and $y$ components. Sum all $x$-components → $F_{net,x}$. Sum all $y$-components → $F_{net,y}$. Draw $\vec{F}_{net}$ on the diagram.
  5. Step 5 — Write Newton's Law: $F = ma$. Write it for EACH axis separately: $F_{net,x} = ma_x$ and $F_{net,y} = ma_y$. Usually one axis has $a = 0$ (no acceleration in that direction).
  6. Step 6 — Derive the asked value. Solve the equations from Step 5 for whatever the problem asks (acceleration, tension, normal force, etc.).
CRITICAL RULES:
1. Only draw forces that act ON the object (not forces the object exerts on others).
2. $mg\sin\theta$ and $mg\cos\theta$ are NOT forces — they are components of gravity that appear AFTER you tilt your axes in Step 3. Never draw them as separate arrows in Step 1.
3. Normal force is perpendicular to the SURFACE, not perpendicular to the ground. On an incline, $N$ is tilted.
4. On inclines: tilt axes so $x$ is along the slope → then $N$ is purely in $y$-direction, and only $mg$ needs decomposing: $mg\sin\theta$ along $x$, $mg\cos\theta$ along $y$.
