CIRCULAR MOTION REFRESHER

AP Physics C: Mechanics • All 6 Problem Types • With AP Strategy Tips
The One Equation That Rules Them All
$\displaystyle \sum F_{\text{toward center}} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r$
This is NOT a new force. It's just $F = ma$ with $a = v^2/r$ pointed inward.

1 Car on Flat Curve

Setup: Car turns on flat road. Friction provides the centripetal force.
$f_s = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$
Max speed before sliding:   $\mu_s mg = \dfrac{mv^2_{max}}{r}$  →  $v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu_s g r}$
AP asks: "What's the max speed?" or "What's the minimum $\mu$?"

2 Banked Curve (No Friction)

Setup: Road tilted at angle $\theta$, designed so no friction is needed.
The normal force has two components:
$N\cos\theta = mg$ (vertical balance)
$N\sin\theta = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$ (centripetal)
$\tan\theta = \dfrac{v^2}{rg}$
AP asks: "At what angle is no friction needed?" or "What's the design speed?"

3 Vertical Circle — Top and Bottom HIGHEST FREQUENCY

At the BOTTOM (ball on string, car in valley): $N$ points up (toward center), $mg$ points down (away)
$N - mg = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$  →  $N = mg + \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$
You feel heavier — $N > mg$
At the TOP (ball on string, car over hill): Both $N$ and $mg$ point down (toward center)
$mg + N = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$  →  $N = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} - mg$
You feel lighter — $N < mg$
Critical speed at the top (when $N = 0$, about to lose contact):   $v_{min} = \sqrt{gr}$
AP loves: "Find minimum speed at the top of the loop" or "Find the normal force at the bottom"

4 Conical Pendulum

Setup: Mass on string swings in a horizontal circle, string makes angle $\theta$ with vertical.
$T\cos\theta = mg$ (vertical)
$T\sin\theta = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$ (centripetal)
Where $r = L\sin\theta$  ($L$ = string length)
$\tan\theta = \dfrac{v^2}{rg}$
$T = \dfrac{mg}{\cos\theta}$
Period:   $T_{\text{period}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{L\cos\theta}{g}}$
AP asks: "Find the tension" or "Find the period" or "What happens if speed increases?" ($\theta$ increases, string gets more horizontal)

5 Satellite / Gravitational Orbit

Setup: Gravity provides the centripetal force.
$\dfrac{GMm}{r^2} = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$
$v_{\text{orbit}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{GM}{r}}$
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{r^3}{GM}}$
Key insights: Higher orbit → slower speed (counterintuitive). Speed doesn't depend on satellite mass. $T^2 \propto r^3$ (Kepler's 3rd law).
AP asks: "If radius doubles, how does period change?" → $T' = T \cdot 2\sqrt{2}$

6 Object on Rotating Platform

Setup: Mass sits on a turntable/disk, friction keeps it in place.
$f_s = m\omega^2 r$
Max radius:   $\mu_s mg = m\omega^2 r_{max}$  →  $r_{max} = \dfrac{\mu_s g}{\omega^2}$
AP asks: "How far from center can the object sit without sliding?"

The Universal Method (Every Problem)

  1. Draw FBD — real forces only (gravity, normal, tension, friction)
  2. Set up axes — one axis points toward the center
  3. Write:   $\sum F_{\text{toward center}} = mv^2/r$
  4. Solve for the unknown

Critical Gotchas

Common TrapCorrect Thinking
"Centripetal force" is a separate forceNo — it's the NET inward force from real forces
"Centrifugal force" pushes outwardNot in an inertial frame — objects just want to go straight
$N = mg$ alwaysNot in circular motion — $N$ depends on position and speed
Faster = flies outwardFaster = needs MORE inward force; if unavailable, goes straight (tangent)
At the top, only gravity actsOnly true at minimum speed ($N = 0$). Otherwise $N + mg = mv^2/r$
