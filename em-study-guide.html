UNIT 1: ELECTROSTATICS

1 Electric Charge & Coulomb's Law

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. There are two types: positive (protons) and negative (electrons). Like charges repel; opposite charges attract. Charge is quantized in units of $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C and is always conserved.

$F = k\dfrac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\dfrac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$

where $k = 8.99 \times 10^9$ N$\cdot$m$^2$/C$^2$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C$^2$/(N$\cdot$m$^2$).

Superposition: The force on a charge from multiple other charges is the vector sum of the individual forces. You must add forces as vectors (components), not magnitudes.

Coulomb's law looks like gravity: $F = kq_1q_2/r^2$ vs. $F = Gm_1m_2/r^2$. Same $1/r^2$ structure, but electric force can attract OR repel (gravity only attracts), and electric force is vastly stronger.
Direction matters. $F = kq_1q_2/r^2$ gives magnitude only. You must determine direction separately: toward or away based on sign of charges.

2 Electric Field

The electric field $\vec{E}$ at a point in space tells you the force per unit positive charge that would be felt by a test charge placed there. It's the "influence" a charge creates around itself.

$\vec{E} = \dfrac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$ (definition)
$\vec{E} = k\dfrac{q}{r^2}\,\hat{r}$ (point charge)

Field lines: Start on positive charges, end on negative charges. Density of lines represents field strength. Lines never cross. The field at any point is tangent to the line through that point.

Superposition: $\vec{E}_{total} = \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 + \cdots$ — add field contributions as vectors.

Think of E-field as a map. Once you know $\vec{E}$ everywhere, you can find the force on ANY charge placed there: $\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$. Positive charges feel force in the direction of $\vec{E}$; negative charges feel force opposite to $\vec{E}$.

3 Continuous Charge Distributions

When charge is spread over an object (not a point charge), you must integrate. Cut the object into tiny pieces $dq$, find the field $d\vec{E}$ from each piece, and add them up.

$\vec{E} = \int \dfrac{k\,dq}{r^2}\,\hat{r}$

Charge densities:

$\lambda = \dfrac{Q}{L}$ (linear, C/m)
$\sigma = \dfrac{Q}{A}$ (surface, C/m$^2$)
$\rho = \dfrac{Q}{V}$ (volume, C/m$^3$)

Key results to know:

ShapeE-field on axis
Infinite line (charge/length $\lambda$)$E = \dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$ (radially outward)
Ring (radius $R$, total charge $Q$, at distance $x$ on axis)$E = \dfrac{kQx}{(x^2+R^2)^{3/2}}$
Infinite plane (charge/area $\sigma$)$E = \dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$ (uniform, independent of distance)
AP tip: On FRQs, they often ask you to SET UP the integral (choose $dq$, express $r$, find limits) even if you don't solve it. Show every step of the setup for full credit.

4 Gauss's Law

$\displaystyle\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \dfrac{q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$

What it says: The total electric flux (field times area) through any closed surface equals the enclosed charge divided by $\varepsilon_0$. This is always true, but only useful when there's enough symmetry to pull $E$ out of the integral.

Electric flux: $\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$. Think of it as "how much field passes through the surface." $d\vec{A}$ points outward from the closed surface.

When to use Gauss's Law (the symmetry test)

  • Spherical symmetry (point charge, solid sphere, shell) → use a spherical Gaussian surface
  • Cylindrical symmetry (infinite line, infinite cylinder) → use a cylindrical Gaussian surface
  • Planar symmetry (infinite plane, parallel plates) → use a "pillbox" (short cylinder straddling the surface)
Gauss's Law gives $E$ only when symmetry lets you argue that $E$ is constant on your Gaussian surface. For arbitrary shapes, Gauss's law is true but not useful for finding $E$ — you'd need Coulomb's law integration instead.

6 Electric Potential

Electric potential $V$ is the electric potential energy per unit charge. It's a scalar (not a vector!), which makes it much easier to work with than $\vec{E}$.

$V = \dfrac{U}{q_0} = \dfrac{kq}{r}$ (point charge)
$V = -\displaystyle\int_{\infty}^{r} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}$ (from E-field)

Potential difference (voltage): $\Delta V = V_B - V_A = -\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}$. This is what batteries provide and what voltmeters measure.

Superposition: $V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + \cdots$ — just add scalars! No vector components needed. This is why potential is often easier than field.

Equipotential surfaces are surfaces where $V$ is constant. They are always perpendicular to field lines. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential surface.
$V$ can be positive, negative, or zero. $V = 0$ does NOT mean $E = 0$. (Example: midpoint between $+q$ and $-q$ has $V = 0$ but $E \neq 0$.)

7 E-Field ↔ Potential Relationship

$\vec{E}$ and $V$ are two descriptions of the same thing. You can always go from one to the other.
E from V:
$E_x = -\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial x}$
E points from high V to low V
V from E:
$\Delta V = -\displaystyle\int \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l}$
Integrate along any path

In 1D (field only in $x$-direction): $E = -dV/dx$. The E-field is the negative slope of the $V$ vs. $x$ graph.

Steep slope on $V$-vs-$x$ graph = strong field. Flat region = no field. $E$ points in the direction of decreasing $V$ (like a ball rolls downhill).
AP loves: "Given $V(x) = 3x^2 - 5x$, find $E_x$." Answer: $E_x = -dV/dx = -(6x - 5) = -6x + 5$.

8 Conductors in Electrostatics

In a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium (charges not moving):

The 5 Rules of Conductors

  1. $E = 0$ inside the conductor (if $E \neq 0$, charges would move — not equilibrium)
  2. All excess charge sits on the surface (Gauss's law: if $E = 0$ inside, $q_{enc} = 0$ for any interior surface)
  3. $E$ is perpendicular to the surface just outside (any parallel component would move surface charges)
  4. $V$ is constant throughout the conductor and its surface (since $E = 0$ inside, no potential difference)
  5. Charge concentrates at sharp points (small radius of curvature = higher surface charge density = stronger field)
Just outside a conductor: $E = \dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}$
Shielding (Faraday cage): A hollow conductor shields its interior from external fields. No matter what's outside, $E = 0$ inside the cavity (as long as there's no charge in the cavity).
UNIT 2: CAPACITORS & DIELECTRICS

9 Capacitance

A capacitor stores charge (and energy) by maintaining a potential difference between two conductors. Capacitance measures how much charge it can store per volt.

$C = \dfrac{Q}{V}$ (definition, units: Farads)
$C = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$ (parallel plate)

Parallel plate capacitor: Two plates of area $A$ separated by distance $d$. The field between them is uniform: $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0 = V/d$.

Bigger plates = more capacitance (more area to store charge). Closer plates = more capacitance (same charge creates larger $E$, hence larger $V$... wait, actually closer plates means less $V$ for same $Q$, so $C = Q/V$ is larger). Think: $C \propto A/d$.

10 Capacitors in Series & Parallel

Series:
$\dfrac{1}{C_{eq}} = \dfrac{1}{C_1} + \dfrac{1}{C_2} + \cdots$
Same $Q$, voltages add
Parallel:
$C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \cdots$
Same $V$, charges add
Opposite of resistors! Capacitors in series combine like resistors in parallel (and vice versa). Easy to mix up on the AP.
Series: $C_{eq}$ is always LESS than the smallest individual capacitor. Parallel: $C_{eq}$ is the SUM (always bigger).

11 Energy Stored in Capacitors

$U = \dfrac{1}{2}CV^2$
$U = \dfrac{Q^2}{2C}$
$U = \dfrac{1}{2}QV$

All three are equivalent (use $Q = CV$ to convert). Choose the form based on what's constant in the problem.

AP classic: "A capacitor is charged, then disconnected from the battery. The plates are pulled apart. What happens to $V$, $E$, $U$?" Since $Q$ is constant (disconnected): $C$ decreases, $V = Q/C$ increases, $U = Q^2/2C$ increases. The extra energy comes from the work YOU do pulling the plates apart.

12 Dielectrics

A dielectric is an insulating material inserted between capacitor plates. It gets polarized by the field, creating an internal field that partially cancels the external field.

$C = \kappa C_0 = \kappa\dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$
$E = \dfrac{E_0}{\kappa}$ (field reduced)

$\kappa$ (kappa) is the dielectric constant. Always $\kappa \ge 1$. Vacuum: $\kappa = 1$.

Effect of inserting dielectric:
• Battery connected (constant $V$): $C$ increases, $Q = CV$ increases, $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$ increases
• Battery disconnected (constant $Q$): $C$ increases, $V = Q/C$ decreases, $U = Q^2/2C$ decreases

13 Energy Density of Electric Field

$u_E = \dfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$ (energy per unit volume, J/m$^3$)

The energy stored in a capacitor is actually stored in the electric field between the plates. This concept generalizes: wherever there's an electric field, there's energy density $u_E$.

Total energy: $U = \int u_E\,dV = \int \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2\,dV$ (integrate over all space where $E$ exists).
UNIT 3: ELECTRIC CIRCUITS

14 Current, Resistance & Ohm's Law

$I = \dfrac{dQ}{dt}$ (current, Amperes)
$V = IR$ (Ohm's law)
$R = \dfrac{\rho L}{A}$ (resistance)

Current is the flow of charge. Conventional current flows from $+$ to $-$ (opposite to electron flow). $I$ = charge per second passing a point.

Resistance depends on the material ($\rho$ = resistivity), length $L$, and cross-sectional area $A$. Longer wire = more resistance. Thicker wire = less resistance.

Water pipe analogy: Voltage = water pressure, current = flow rate, resistance = pipe narrowness. Higher pressure (voltage) drives more flow (current). Narrow pipe (high resistance) limits flow.

15 Resistors in Series & Parallel

Series:
$R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots$
Same $I$, voltages add
Parallel:
$\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \cdots$
Same $V$, currents add
Series: Current is the same through all resistors (only one path). Voltage drops add up to the battery voltage.
Parallel: Voltage is the same across all resistors (connected to same two nodes). Currents split and add back together.

16 Kirchhoff's Rules

Junction Rule:
$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$
Conservation of charge
Loop Rule:
$\sum \Delta V = 0$
Conservation of energy

How to apply Kirchhoff's Loop Rule

  1. Choose a loop direction (clockwise or counterclockwise)
  2. Going through a battery from $-$ to $+$: voltage RISES by $+\mathcal{E}$
  3. Going through a battery from $+$ to $-$: voltage DROPS by $-\mathcal{E}$
  4. Going through a resistor in the direction of current: voltage DROPS by $-IR$
  5. Going through a resistor against the current: voltage RISES by $+IR$
  6. Sum all changes around the loop = 0
AP tip: For circuits with two batteries or complex branching, you MUST use Kirchhoff's rules. Write one equation per loop and one per junction. Solve the system of equations.

17 RC Circuits

When a capacitor charges or discharges through a resistor, the current changes exponentially with time constant $\tau = RC$.

Charging ($V_0$ = battery):
$q(t) = CV_0\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right)$
$I(t) = \dfrac{V_0}{R}\,e^{-t/RC}$
$V_C(t) = V_0\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right)$
Discharging:
$q(t) = Q_0\,e^{-t/RC}$
$I(t) = \dfrac{Q_0}{RC}\,e^{-t/RC}$
$V_C(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/RC}$
Time constant: $\tau = RC$. After $1\tau$: 63% charged (or 37% remaining). After $5\tau$: essentially complete.
At $t = 0$: An uncharged capacitor acts like a wire (no voltage across it, max current). At $t = \infty$: A fully charged capacitor acts like an open circuit (no current, max voltage).
Current during charging DECREASES exponentially (starts high, decays to zero). Charge and voltage INCREASE exponentially toward their final values.

18 Power in Circuits

$P = IV$
$P = I^2R$
$P = \dfrac{V^2}{R}$

Power is the rate of energy dissipation (in resistors) or energy delivery (by batteries). All three forms are equivalent via $V = IR$.

In series: Same $I$, so the resistor with the largest $R$ dissipates the most power ($P = I^2R$).
In parallel: Same $V$, so the resistor with the smallest $R$ dissipates the most power ($P = V^2/R$).
UNIT 4: MAGNETIC FIELDS

19 Magnetic Force on Moving Charges

$\vec{F} = q\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$   →   $F = |q|vB\sin\theta$

The magnetic force is always perpendicular to both $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$. This means it NEVER does work (doesn't change speed, only direction).

Right-Hand Rule for $\vec{F} = q\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$

  1. Point fingers in the direction of $\vec{v}$
  2. Curl fingers toward $\vec{B}$
  3. Thumb points in the direction of $\vec{F}$ (for positive charge)
  4. For negative charges, flip the direction of $\vec{F}$
If $\vec{v}$ is parallel to $\vec{B}$: $\sin\theta = 0$, so $F = 0$. A charge moving along a field line feels no force. Maximum force when $\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$.

20 Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

Since $\vec{F}$ is always perpendicular to $\vec{v}$, a charged particle in a uniform $\vec{B}$ field moves in a circle (if $\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$) or a helix (if there's a component along $\vec{B}$).

$r = \dfrac{mv}{|q|B}$ (radius of circular orbit)
$T = \dfrac{2\pi m}{|q|B}$ (period — independent of speed!)
Faster particle = bigger circle (but same period). This is the basis of the cyclotron: particles spiral outward as they accelerate, but always take the same time per revolution.
AP classic: "A proton and an electron enter the same B field with the same speed. Compare their radii." The proton has $\sim 1836\times$ more mass, so its radius is $\sim 1836\times$ bigger.

21 Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

$\vec{F} = I\vec{L} \times \vec{B}$   →   $F = BIL\sin\theta$

$\vec{L}$ points in the direction of current flow. The force on the wire is perpendicular to both $\vec{L}$ and $\vec{B}$. Use the same right-hand rule as before, but with $I\vec{L}$ replacing $q\vec{v}$.

Two parallel wires: Currents in the same direction attract. Currents in opposite directions repel. (Each wire's field exerts a force on the other wire.)

22 Biot-Savart Law

Biot-Savart tells you the magnetic field created by a current-carrying wire. It's the magnetic analog of Coulomb's law.

$d\vec{B} = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{I\,d\vec{l} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T$\cdot$m/A. Integrate over the wire to get total $\vec{B}$.

Key results:

SourceB-field
Long straight wire (distance $r$)$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$
Center of circular loop (radius $R$)$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$
On axis of loop (distance $x$)$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}}$
Direction: Use the right-hand rule for wires. Curl your right hand in the direction of current flow; your thumb points in the direction of $\vec{B}$ (for a loop) or curl fingers around the wire (for a straight wire).

23 Ampère's Law

$\displaystyle\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc}$

Ampère's Law is to magnetism what Gauss's Law is to electrostatics. It relates the line integral of $\vec{B}$ around a closed loop to the current passing through the loop. Like Gauss's Law, it's always true but only useful with high symmetry.

Key applications:

ConfigurationAmperian LoopResult
Long straight wireCircle around wire$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$
Solenoid (inside)Rectangle through coil$B = \mu_0 n I$ ($n$ = turns/length)
Solenoid (outside)—$B \approx 0$
Toroid (inside)Circle inside toroid$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r}$
Solenoid: A coil of wire wound tightly. Inside: uniform $B = \mu_0 n I$ (strong, uniform field). Outside: $B \approx 0$. It's the magnetic analog of a parallel-plate capacitor (uniform field in a region).

24 Magnetic Flux

$\Phi_B = \int \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = BA\cos\theta$ (uniform $B$, flat area)

Magnetic flux measures "how much magnetic field passes through a surface." $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{B}$ and the normal to the surface. Units: Weber (Wb) = T$\cdot$m$^2$.

Maximum flux: when $\vec{B}$ is perpendicular to the surface ($\theta = 0°$, field goes straight through). Zero flux: when $\vec{B}$ is parallel to the surface ($\theta = 90°$, field skims along the surface).
Gauss's Law for magnetism: $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0$ (no magnetic monopoles). Net flux through any closed surface is always zero.
UNIT 5: ELECTROMAGNETIC INDUCTION

25 Faraday's Law

$\mathcal{E} = -\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$

A changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an EMF (voltage). This is the foundation of generators, transformers, and induction cooktops.

Three ways to change flux ($\Phi = BA\cos\theta$):

Change $B$
(field strengthens/weakens)
Change $A$
(loop expands/shrinks)
Change $\theta$
(loop rotates)

For $N$ loops: $\mathcal{E} = -N\dfrac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$

Motional EMF: A conducting rod of length $L$ moving at speed $v$ perpendicular to a field $B$ generates: $\mathcal{E} = BLv$. This is Faraday's law applied to a changing area.
AP FRQ favorite: A rectangular loop enters/exits a region of uniform $B$. Find the EMF, current, and force as functions of position.

26 Lenz's Law

The induced current flows in a direction that OPPOSES the change in flux that caused it.

Lenz's law is the reason for the negative sign in Faraday's law. It's a consequence of energy conservation — if the induced current aided the change, you'd get runaway energy from nothing.

How to Apply Lenz's Law

  1. Determine if the flux through the loop is increasing or decreasing
  2. The induced current creates a B-field that opposes the change:
    • Flux increasing? Induced B opposes the external B (induced current creates B in opposite direction)
    • Flux decreasing? Induced B supports the external B (tries to maintain it)
  3. Use the right-hand rule to find which current direction produces the needed induced B
Quick test: If a magnet's north pole approaches a loop, the loop creates its own north pole facing the magnet (to repel and oppose the approach). If the magnet retreats, the loop creates a south pole (to attract and oppose the retreat).

27 Inductance

An inductor is a coil of wire that opposes changes in current (just as a capacitor opposes changes in voltage). When the current through an inductor changes, it induces an EMF that resists the change.

$\mathcal{E} = -L\dfrac{dI}{dt}$ (self-induced EMF)
$L = \mu_0 n^2 A \ell$ (solenoid, $\ell$ = length)

$L$ is the inductance, measured in Henries (H). It depends on the geometry of the coil.

Analogy: An inductor is like a heavy flywheel. It resists changes in current (like a flywheel resists changes in rotation speed). Once current is flowing, the inductor tries to keep it flowing.

28 LR Circuits

When an inductor and resistor are connected to a battery, the current grows exponentially (the inductor opposes sudden changes).

Energizing:
$I(t) = \dfrac{\mathcal{E}}{R}\left(1 - e^{-Rt/L}\right)$
$V_L(t) = \mathcal{E}\,e^{-Rt/L}$
De-energizing:
$I(t) = I_0\,e^{-Rt/L}$
$V_L(t) = -I_0 R\,e^{-Rt/L}$
Time constant: $\tau = L/R$. After $1\tau$: 63% of final current. After $5\tau$: essentially complete.
At $t = 0$: An inductor with no initial current acts like an open circuit (blocks sudden current). At $t = \infty$: An inductor acts like a plain wire (no voltage drop, full current).
Compare with RC circuits: In RC, current STARTS high and decays. In LR, current STARTS at zero and grows. They are "mirror images" of each other. The time constant is $\tau = RC$ for capacitors and $\tau = L/R$ for inductors.

29 Energy in Inductors & Magnetic Fields

$U = \dfrac{1}{2}LI^2$ (energy in inductor)
$u_B = \dfrac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$ (energy density of B field)

Just as a capacitor stores energy in its electric field ($U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$, $u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$), an inductor stores energy in its magnetic field.

Beautiful symmetry between E and B:
ElectricMagnetic
$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$$U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$
$u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$$u_B = \dfrac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$
$\tau = RC$$\tau = L/R$
Capacitor: opposes $\Delta V$Inductor: opposes $\Delta I$
$t=0$: wire (no $V$)$t=0$: open (no $I$)
$t=\infty$: open (full $V$)$t=\infty$: wire (full $I$)

Exam-Day Decision Tree

  • Finding E-field from charge distribution? → Coulomb/integration (any shape) or Gauss's law (symmetric shapes)
  • Finding potential? → $V = kq/r$ (point charges) or $V = -\int \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l}$ (from known E)
  • Capacitor problem? → $C = Q/V$, check what's constant (battery connected vs disconnected)
  • Circuit with batteries and resistors? → Kirchhoff's rules
  • Circuit with capacitor? → RC exponentials, $\tau = RC$
  • Force on charge in B field? → $F = qvB\sin\theta$, right-hand rule
  • Finding B-field from current? → Biot-Savart (any shape) or Ampère's law (symmetric shapes)
  • Changing flux? → Faraday's law $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi/dt$, Lenz's law for direction
  • Circuit with inductor? → LR exponentials, $\tau = L/R$

SAMPLE QUESTIONS

2 per topic • Most likely AP exam types • With answers

Unit 1: Electrostatics
Q1. Two charges $+3q$ and $-q$ are separated by distance $d$. The electric field is zero at a point:
(A) Between them, closer to $-q$(B) Between them, at the midpoint(C) Outside, beyond $-q$(D) Outside, beyond $+3q$(E) Nowhere
Answer: (C) — The zero-field point must be outside, beyond the weaker charge $(-q)$, where the fields from both charges point in opposite directions and can cancel. Between them, both fields point the same way (toward $-q$).
Q2. A solid insulating sphere of radius $R$ has uniform charge $Q$. The electric field at $r = R/2$ is:
(A) $kQ/R^2$(B) $kQ/(2R^2)$(C) $kQ/(4R^2)$(D) $2kQ/R^2$(E) $4kQ/R^2$
Answer: (B) — Inside a uniform sphere, $E = kQr/R^3$. At $r = R/2$: $E = kQ(R/2)/R^3 = kQ/(2R^2)$.
Q3. A Gaussian sphere of radius $r$ surrounds a point charge $+Q$. If the radius is doubled, the electric flux through the surface:
(A) Quadruples(B) Doubles(C) Stays the same(D) Halves(E) Quarters
Answer: (C) — Gauss's law: $\Phi = q_{enc}/\varepsilon_0$. The enclosed charge hasn't changed, so the flux is the same regardless of the Gaussian surface size.
Q4. The electric potential at the center of a square with charges $+q$ at each corner (side length $a$) is:
(A) $0$(B) $4kq/a$(C) $4\sqrt{2}\,kq/a$(D) $kq/a$(E) $2kq/a$
Answer: (C) — Distance from each corner to center $= a\sqrt{2}/2$. $V = 4 \times kq/(a\sqrt{2}/2) = 4 \times 2kq/(a\sqrt{2}) = 4\sqrt{2}\,kq/a$. Potential is scalar — just add!
Q5. $V(x) = 5x^2 - 3x$ (in volts, $x$ in meters). The $x$-component of the electric field at $x = 2$ m is:
(A) $14$ V/m(B) $-17$ V/m(C) $17$ V/m(D) $-14$ V/m(E) $-17$ N/C
Answer: (B) — $E_x = -dV/dx = -(10x - 3)$. At $x = 2$: $E_x = -(20 - 3) = -17$ V/m.
Q6. A conducting sphere of radius $R$ carries charge $Q$. The electric field just outside the surface is $E_0$. The field at $r = 2R$ is:
(A) $E_0$(B) $E_0/2$(C) $E_0/4$(D) $2E_0$(E) $4E_0$
Answer: (C) — Outside a sphere, $E \propto 1/r^2$. At $r = 2R$: $E = E_0(R/2R)^2 = E_0/4$.
Unit 2: Capacitors & Dielectrics
Q7. A parallel-plate capacitor ($C = 10\;\mu$F) is charged to $V = 100$ V, then disconnected. If the plate separation is doubled, the new voltage is:
(A) $50$ V(B) $100$ V(C) $200$ V(D) $400$ V(E) $25$ V
Answer: (C) — Disconnected: $Q$ is constant. Doubling $d$ halves $C$. $V = Q/C$ doubles to $200$ V.
Q8. Three $6\;\mu$F capacitors are connected in series. The equivalent capacitance is:
(A) $18\;\mu$F(B) $6\;\mu$F(C) $3\;\mu$F(D) $2\;\mu$F(E) $1\;\mu$F
Answer: (D) — $1/C_{eq} = 1/6 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 3/6 = 1/2$. $C_{eq} = 2\;\mu$F. Series always gives less than the smallest.
Q9. A capacitor stores energy $U$. If the voltage is tripled (capacitance unchanged), the new energy is:
(A) $U$(B) $3U$(C) $6U$(D) $9U$(E) $U/3$
Answer: (D) — $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$. Tripling $V$ gives $U' = \frac{1}{2}C(3V)^2 = 9 \times \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = 9U$.
Q10. A dielectric with $\kappa = 3$ is inserted into a capacitor while it remains connected to a battery. The charge on the capacitor:
(A) Stays the same(B) Triples(C) Is divided by 3(D) Doubles(E) Goes to zero
Answer: (B) — Battery connected: $V$ is constant. $C$ triples (dielectric). $Q = CV$ triples. More charge flows from the battery.
Unit 3: Circuits
Q11. A wire of resistance $R$ is stretched to twice its length (volume constant). Its new resistance is:
(A) $R/2$(B) $R$(C) $2R$(D) $4R$(E) $R/4$
Answer: (D) — $R = \rho L/A$. Doubling $L$ halves $A$ (constant volume: $LA = $ const). New $R = \rho(2L)/(A/2) = 4\rho L/A = 4R$.
Q12. Two resistors $R_1 = 3\;\Omega$ and $R_2 = 6\;\Omega$ are in parallel across a $12$ V battery. The total current from the battery is:
(A) $2$ A(B) $4$ A(C) $6$ A(D) $1.3$ A(E) $8$ A
Answer: (C) — $R_{eq} = (3 \times 6)/(3+6) = 2\;\Omega$. $I = V/R_{eq} = 12/2 = 6$ A.
Q13. In a loop with a $12$ V battery and resistors $3\;\Omega$ and $5\;\Omega$ in series, plus an unknown EMF $\mathcal{E}$, the current is $1$ A clockwise. Applying Kirchhoff's loop rule: $12 - 3(1) - 5(1) - \mathcal{E} = 0$. The unknown EMF is:
(A) $20$ V(B) $4$ V(C) $8$ V(D) $-4$ V(E) $0$ V
Answer: (B) — $12 - 3 - 5 - \mathcal{E} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathcal{E} = 4$ V.
Q14. An RC circuit has $R = 5\;$k$\Omega$ and $C = 2\;\mu$F. The time constant is:
(A) $10$ s(B) $0.01$ s(C) $10$ ms(D) $0.1$ s(E) $2.5$ ms
Answer: (B) — $\tau = RC = 5000 \times 2 \times 10^{-6} = 0.01$ s $= 10$ ms. (Both C and B could be argued equivalent — $0.01$ s $= 10$ ms.)
Q15. In an RC circuit, a capacitor is fully charged to $Q_0$ then discharged. After $2\tau$, the charge remaining is approximately:
(A) $0.37 Q_0$(B) $0.13 Q_0$(C) $0.05 Q_0$(D) $0.63 Q_0$(E) $0.50 Q_0$
Answer: (B) — $q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau}$. At $t = 2\tau$: $q = Q_0 e^{-2} \approx 0.135 Q_0 \approx 0.13 Q_0$.
Q16. Two identical light bulbs ($R$ each) are in parallel across a battery. One burns out (open circuit). The power dissipated by the remaining bulb:
(A) Doubles(B) Halves(C) Stays the same(D) Quadruples(E) Goes to zero
Answer: (C) — In parallel, each bulb has the full battery voltage across it regardless of the other. So $P = V^2/R$ is unchanged for the surviving bulb.
Unit 4: Magnetism
Q17. A proton moves at $2 \times 10^6$ m/s perpendicular to a $0.5$ T magnetic field. The radius of its circular path is ($m_p = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C):
(A) $0.042$ m(B) $0.42$ m(C) $4.2$ m(D) $0.0042$ m(E) $42$ m
Answer: (A) — $r = mv/(qB) = (1.67 \times 10^{-27})(2 \times 10^6)/((1.6 \times 10^{-19})(0.5)) = 3.34 \times 10^{-21}/(8 \times 10^{-20}) \approx 0.042$ m.
Q18. A positive charge moves north in a magnetic field pointing east. The magnetic force on the charge is:
(A) North(B) South(C) Up(D) Down(E) West
Answer: (C) — Right-hand rule: fingers point north ($\vec{v}$), curl toward east ($\vec{B}$), thumb points UP. $\vec{F} = q\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$.
Q19. A $2$ m wire carrying $5$ A is in a $0.3$ T uniform field at $90°$ to the wire. The force on the wire is:
(A) $0.3$ N(B) $3$ N(C) $30$ N(D) $1.5$ N(E) $0.6$ N
Answer: (B) — $F = BIL\sin\theta = (0.3)(5)(2)\sin 90° = 3$ N.
Q20. The magnetic field at distance $r = 0.1$ m from a long straight wire carrying $10$ A is:
(A) $2 \times 10^{-5}$ T(B) $2 \times 10^{-6}$ T(C) $2 \times 10^{-4}$ T(D) $4\pi \times 10^{-5}$ T(E) $10^{-5}$ T
Answer: (A) — $B = \mu_0 I/(2\pi r) = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(10)/(2\pi \times 0.1) = 2 \times 10^{-5}$ T.
Q21. A solenoid has $500$ turns over $0.25$ m length and carries $4$ A. The magnetic field inside is:
(A) $1.0 \times 10^{-2}$ T(B) $5.0 \times 10^{-3}$ T(C) $1.6 \times 10^{-2}$ T(D) $3.2 \times 10^{-3}$ T(E) $8.0 \times 10^{-3}$ T
Answer: (A) — $n = 500/0.25 = 2000$ turns/m. $B = \mu_0 n I = (4\pi \times 10^{-7})(2000)(4) \approx 1.005 \times 10^{-2}$ T.
Q22. A uniform $B = 0.4$ T passes through a circular loop of radius $0.1$ m at $60°$ to the normal. The magnetic flux is:
(A) $0.004\pi$ Wb(B) $0.002\pi$ Wb(C) $0.04\pi$ Wb(D) $0.001\pi$ Wb(E) $0.008\pi$ Wb
Answer: (B) — $\Phi = BA\cos\theta = (0.4)(\pi \times 0.01)\cos 60° = 0.004\pi \times 0.5 = 0.002\pi$ Wb.
Unit 5: Electromagnetic Induction
Q23. A circular loop of area $0.05$ m$^2$ is in a magnetic field that changes from $0.2$ T to $0.8$ T in $0.1$ s. The magnitude of the induced EMF is:
(A) $0.3$ V(B) $3$ V(C) $0.03$ V(D) $30$ V(E) $0.6$ V
Answer: (A) — $|\mathcal{E}| = |d\Phi/dt| = A \cdot |\Delta B/\Delta t| = 0.05 \times (0.6/0.1) = 0.05 \times 6 = 0.3$ V.
Q24. A rod of length $0.5$ m moves at $4$ m/s perpendicular to a $0.6$ T field. The motional EMF is:
(A) $0.12$ V(B) $1.2$ V(C) $12$ V(D) $0.3$ V(E) $4.8$ V
Answer: (B) — $\mathcal{E} = BLv = (0.6)(0.5)(4) = 1.2$ V.
Q25. A magnet is dropped through a horizontal copper ring. As the north pole approaches the ring from above, the induced current (viewed from above) flows:
(A) Clockwise(B) Counterclockwise(C) No current(D) Alternating(E) Depends on ring size
Answer: (B) — Flux (downward) is increasing. By Lenz's law, the induced current opposes the increase by creating an upward B-field. Right-hand rule: counterclockwise viewed from above.
Q26. After the magnet in Q25 passes through the ring and falls below it, the induced current (viewed from above):
(A) Stays counterclockwise(B) Becomes clockwise(C) Stops(D) Reverses twice(E) Depends on speed
Answer: (B) — Now the downward flux is decreasing (magnet moving away below). Lenz's law: current opposes the decrease by maintaining downward flux → clockwise viewed from above.
Q27. A solenoid has inductance $L = 0.2$ H. If the current changes from $3$ A to $1$ A in $0.01$ s, the magnitude of the induced EMF is:
(A) $4$ V(B) $40$ V(C) $0.4$ V(D) $400$ V(E) $0.04$ V
Answer: (B) — $|\mathcal{E}| = L|dI/dt| = 0.2 \times |(-2)/0.01| = 0.2 \times 200 = 40$ V.
Q28. An LR circuit has $L = 50$ mH and $R = 10\;\Omega$. The time constant is:
(A) $0.5$ s(B) $5$ ms(C) $500$ s(D) $0.05$ s(E) $50$ ms
Answer: (B) — $\tau = L/R = 0.050/10 = 0.005$ s $= 5$ ms.
Q29. An inductor with $L = 0.1$ H carries $4$ A. The energy stored is:
(A) $0.2$ J(B) $0.4$ J(C) $0.8$ J(D) $1.6$ J(E) $8$ J
Answer: (C) — $U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.1)(16) = 0.8$ J.
Q30. In a region where $B = 0.02$ T, the energy density of the magnetic field is ($\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$):
(A) $160$ J/m$^3$(B) $0.16$ J/m$^3$(C) $1.6$ J/m$^3$(D) $0.016$ J/m$^3$(E) $16$ J/m$^3$
Answer: (B) — $u = B^2/(2\mu_0) = (0.0004)/(2 \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7}) = (4 \times 10^{-4})/(8\pi \times 10^{-7}) \approx 0.16$ J/m$^3$.
