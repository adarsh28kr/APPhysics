Category A: Flat Surface + Forces (Q1-5)
Q1Answer: (A) $2$ m/s$^2$
A 8 kg crate is pushed across a rough floor ($\mu_k = 0.3$) by two horizontal forces: 50 N to the right and 10 N to the left. The acceleration is:
Step 1 — Normal force: Flat surface, no vertical applied forces: $N = mg = 8 \times 10 = 80$ N
Step 2 — Friction: $f_k = \mu_k N = 0.3 \times 80 = 24$ N (opposing motion, leftward)
Step 3 — Net force: $F_{net} = 50 - 10 - 24 = 16$ N (rightward)
Step 4 — Acceleration: $a = F_{net}/m = 16/8 = 2$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: Always compute friction FIRST. Write $f_k = \mu_k mg$ as your very first line. Then list ALL horizontal forces with signs. Students who rush skip friction and get $a = (50-10)/8 = 5$ m/s$^2$ — that's choice (B), the classic trap.
Gotcha: Three horizontal forces, not two! You must subtract BOTH the opposing 10 N force AND the 24 N friction from the 50 N. Missing any one gives a wrong answer.
Common mistakes: (B) 5 = forgot friction entirely $(40/8)$. (C) 3 = forgot the 10 N opposing force $(50-24)/8$. (E) 7.5 = forgot both friction and the 10 N force somehow.
Q2Answer: (B) $30$ N
A 10 kg box on a rough floor ($\mu_s = 0.4$, $\mu_k = 0.2$) is pushed with 30 N horizontally. The friction force on the box is:
Step 1 — Check: does it move? Max static friction = $\mu_s mg = 0.4 \times 10 \times 10 = 40$ N
Step 2 — Compare: Applied force $= 30$ N $< 40$ N (max static). The box does NOT move.
Step 3 — Static friction: Since the box is stationary, static friction exactly matches the applied force: $f_s = 30$ N
Teacher's approach: This is the #1 friction trap on AP exams. ALWAYS check "does it move?" BEFORE computing acceleration. Step 1: compute max static friction. Step 2: compare to applied force. If applied $<$ max static, the block is stationary and $f_s = $ applied force. Only if applied $>$ max static do you switch to kinetic friction $\mu_k$.
Gotcha: Static friction is NOT always $\mu_s N$. That formula gives the MAXIMUM. If the block isn't on the verge of sliding, $f_s < \mu_s N$. Static friction is whatever it needs to be to prevent motion, up to its maximum.
Common mistakes: (A) 20 N = used kinetic friction $\mu_k mg = 0.2 \times 100$ even though the block isn't moving. (C) 40 N = used max static friction — that's the max, not the actual value. (D) 0 N = forgot friction exists.
Q3Answer: (A) $2.5$ m/s$^2$
A 4 kg object on a frictionless surface has two forces: $F_1 = 20$ N at 30° above horizontal (right) and $F_2 = 7.32$ N horizontally to the left. The horizontal acceleration is:
Step 1 — Decompose $F_1$: $F_{1x} = 20\cos 30° = 20 \times 0.866 = 17.32$ N (right). $F_{1y} = 20\sin 30° = 10$ N (up).
Step 2 — Net horizontal: $F_{net,x} = 17.32 - 7.32 = 10$ N (right)
Step 3 — Acceleration: $a_x = F_{net,x}/m = 10/4 = 2.5$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: When a force is at an angle, ALWAYS decompose it BEFORE doing anything else. Write $F_x = F\cos\theta$ and $F_y = F\sin\theta$ as separate lines. Then add ALL x-components. Notice the numbers are designed so $17.32 - 7.32 = 10$ N — clean arithmetic confirms your decomposition is correct.
Gotcha: Don't use the full 20 N in the horizontal equation! Only the horizontal component $20\cos 30° = 17.32$ N contributes to horizontal acceleration. Using $20 - 7.32 = 12.68$, then $a = 3.17$ would give choice (D) — wrong.
Common mistakes: (D) 3.17 = used full 20 N instead of horizontal component. (E) 5 = used only $F_1$ with no decomposition: $20/4$. (B) 1.83 = subtracted $F_2$ from $F_{1y}$ instead of $F_{1x}$.
Q4Answer: (A) No — max friction 24 N > weight 20 N
A block is pushed against a vertical wall by a horizontal force of 80 N. The block weighs 20 N. If $\mu_s = 0.3$, does the block slide?
Step 1 — Normal force: Wall pushes back horizontally: $N = 80$ N (horizontal, from wall)
Step 2 — Max static friction: $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = 0.3 \times 80 = 24$ N (vertical, upward)
Step 3 — Compare: Weight $= 20$ N (down). Since $24 > 20$, friction CAN support the block. It does NOT slide.
Teacher's approach: Wall problems flip everything. On a floor: N is vertical, friction is horizontal. On a WALL: N is horizontal, friction is vertical. The question is: can vertical friction support the weight? If $\mu_s N \geq mg$, the block stays. This is a favorite AP conceptual question.
Gotcha: Choice (B) says "max friction 24 N < weight 80 N." That compares friction to the APPLIED force (80 N), not the weight (20 N). The applied force is balanced by the normal force, not by friction. Friction only needs to balance the weight.
Common mistakes: (B) = confused applied force (80 N) with weight (20 N). (C) = "normal force exceeds weight" is irrelevant since N is horizontal. (D) = guessing without computing.
Q5Answer: (A) $0.3$
A force vs. acceleration graph for an object on a rough surface is linear with slope 5 kg and x-intercept at $F = 15$ N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is ($g = 10$ m/s$^2$):
Step 1 — Equation from graph: $F = ma + f_k$. This is linear in $a$ with slope $= m = 5$ kg.
Step 2 — x-intercept: At $a = 0$: $F = f_k = 15$ N. This means friction force is 15 N.
Step 3 — Solve: $f_k = \mu_k mg \Rightarrow 15 = \mu_k \times 5 \times 10 \Rightarrow \mu_k = 0.3$
Teacher's approach: The F-vs-a graph is a straight line. Its slope gives the mass, and the y-intercept (or x-intercept depending on which axis is which) gives the friction force. This is a classic AP lab-style question — linearize the physics, extract parameters from the graph.
Gotcha: Make sure you know which axis is which. If $F$ is on the horizontal axis and $a$ on the vertical, the slope is $1/m$ and the x-intercept gives $f_k$. Here the slope IS given as 5 kg (so $m = 5$) and the x-intercept is $F = 15$ N.
Category B: Inclines (Q6-12)
Q6Answer: (A) $6$ m/s$^2$
A 5 kg block on a frictionless 37° incline ($\sin 37° = 0.6$, $\cos 37° = 0.8$) has acceleration:
Step 1 — Forces: Only $mg$ (down) and $N$ (perpendicular to incline). No friction.
Step 2 — Along incline: $mg\sin 37° = ma$
Step 3 — Solve: $a = g\sin 37° = 10 \times 0.6 = 6$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: The formula $a = g\sin\theta$ should be instant recall for any frictionless incline. Quick sanity checks: (1) At $\theta = 0°$: $a = 0$ ✓ (flat surface). (2) At $\theta = 90°$: $a = g$ ✓ (free fall). (3) Mass doesn't matter ✓.
Gotcha: (B) 8 = $g\cos 37°$. Students mix up sin and cos on inclines. The component ALONG the incline is $g\sin\theta$, not $\cos$. Memory trick: at $\theta = 90°$, "along" = straight down = $g$, and $\sin 90° = 1$ ✓.
Q7Answer: (A) $1/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.577$
A block slides DOWN a 30° rough incline at constant velocity. The coefficient of kinetic friction is:
Step 1 — Key phrase: "Constant velocity" means $a = 0$, so net force $= 0$.
Step 2 — Along incline: $mg\sin 30° = \mu_k mg\cos 30°$ (gravity component down = friction up)
Step 3 — Cancel $mg$: $\mu_k = \frac{\sin 30°}{\cos 30°} = \tan 30° = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.577$
Teacher's approach: Memorize this golden result: $\mu_k = \tan\theta$ when sliding at constant velocity. It appears on AP exams almost every year. This is also how you experimentally measure $\mu_k$ — tilt a board until the block slides at constant speed, then $\mu_k = \tan\theta$.
Gotcha: "Constant velocity" does NOT mean the block is stationary. It means it's MOVING but not accelerating ($a = 0$). Also, mass cancels completely — $\mu_k$ depends only on the angle.
Q8Answer: (A) $12.5$ m/s$^2$
A 4 kg block is PUSHED down a frictionless 30° incline by a force of 30 N applied parallel to the incline. The acceleration is:
Step 1 — Forces along incline (down = +): Gravity component $= mg\sin 30° = 4(10)(0.5) = 20$ N. Applied force $= 30$ N (also down).
Step 2 — Both forces point SAME direction: $F_{net} = 20 + 30 = 50$ N
Step 3 — Acceleration: $a = 50/4 = 12.5$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: The key word is "PUSHED DOWN." Both gravity's component and the applied force point in the same direction (down the incline). They ADD. Always ask: "Do these forces point the same way or opposite?" If pushed UP, you'd subtract instead.
Gotcha: If you subtract instead of add, you'd get $(30-20)/4 = 2.5$ — choice (E). This would only be correct if the force pushed the block UP the incline against gravity.
Q9Answer: (A) $90$ N
Two blocks ($m_1 = 3$ kg, $m_2 = 6$ kg) are in contact on a 30° frictionless incline. A force $F = 135$ N pushes $m_1$ up the incline. The contact force between the blocks is:
Step 1 — System (both blocks together): $F - (m_1+m_2)g\sin 30° = (m_1+m_2)a$
$135 - 9(10)(0.5) = 9a \Rightarrow 135 - 45 = 9a \Rightarrow a = 10$ m/s$^2$
Step 2 — Isolate $m_2$: $C - m_2 g\sin 30° = m_2 a$
$C = m_2(a + g\sin 30°) = 6(10 + 5) = 90$ N
Teacher's approach: For contact force problems: (1) treat the whole system to find $a$, then (2) isolate ONE block to find the contact force. Always isolate the block that does NOT have the external force — it has fewer forces and simpler math.
Q10Answer: (A) $0.095$ m
A 3 kg block on a 53° incline ($\sin 53° = 0.8$, $\cos 53° = 0.6$) is attached to a spring ($k = 300$ N/m) parallel to the surface. If $\mu_s = 0.25$, the max spring compression before the block slides up is:
Step 1 — At threshold (about to slide UP): Spring pushes up the incline. Gravity pulls down. Friction acts DOWN (opposing impending upward motion).
Step 2 — Balance: $kx = mg\sin 53° + \mu_s mg\cos 53°$
Step 3 — Plug in: $300x = 3(10)(0.8) + 0.25 \times 3(10)(0.6) = 24 + 4.5 = 28.5$ N
Step 4 — Solve: $x = 28.5/300 = 0.095$ m
Teacher's approach: When the block is about to slide UP, friction acts DOWN the incline (opposing impending motion). So gravity and friction both resist the spring force. This means the spring needs a LARGER compression to start motion than it would on a frictionless incline.
Gotcha: If the block were about to slide DOWN instead, friction would act UP the incline, and the equation would be $kx = mg\sin\theta - \mu_s mg\cos\theta$. Direction of impending motion determines friction direction!
Q11Answer: (A) $0.51$
A block on a rough incline begins to slide when the angle reaches $27°$. The coefficient of static friction is approximately:
Step 1 — At the verge of sliding: $mg\sin\theta = \mu_s mg\cos\theta$
Step 2 — Solve: $\mu_s = \tan 27° \approx 0.5095 \approx 0.51$
Teacher's approach: This is the experimental method for measuring $\mu_s$: tilt a board until the block JUST starts to slide. At that critical angle, $\mu_s = \tan\theta_{critical}$. Same formula as Q7, but with static friction and the angle of impending motion.
Q12Answer: (A) $0$ N
A 3 kg block on a rough 37° incline ($\mu_k = 0.15$) is connected by a string to a 2 kg block further up the same incline. Both slide down together. The tension is:
Step 1 — Each block independently: $a = g(\sin 37° - \mu_k \cos 37°) = 10(0.6 - 0.15 \times 0.8) = 10(0.48) = 4.8$ m/s$^2$
Step 2 — Key insight: Mass cancels! Both blocks have the SAME acceleration regardless of mass (same $\mu_k$, same $\theta$).
Step 3: Since both accelerate identically, the string is slack. $T = 0$.
Teacher's approach: This is one of the best conceptual questions. Students see "connected by a string" and assume $T > 0$. But if both blocks have the same individual acceleration, they don't pull on each other. Think: two friends running at the same speed holding a rope — no tension!
Gotcha: This ONLY works because both blocks share the same $\mu_k$ AND the same $\theta$. If one block had different friction, $T \neq 0$. The mass cancellation ($a$ independent of $m$ on an incline with friction) is the key insight.
Category C: Connected Systems (Q13-18)
Q13Answer: (A) $1.25$ m/s$^2$
A modified Atwood: 8 kg on frictionless table, 5 kg hanging right, 3 kg hanging left. The acceleration is:
Step 1 — Net driving force: $(m_{right} - m_{left})g = (5 - 3)(10) = 20$ N
Step 2 — Total mass: $m_{total} = 8 + 5 + 3 = 16$ kg (ALL masses accelerate together)
Step 3: $a = 20/16 = 1.25$ m/s$^2$ (toward the heavier hanging mass)
Teacher's approach: For modified Atwood machines: Net force = difference in hanging weights. Total mass = ALL masses (including the one on the table). The table mass doesn't contribute to the driving force, but it DOES add inertia.
Q14Answer: (A) $2.5$ m/s$^2$
Two blocks ($m_1 = 2$ kg and $m_2 = 6$ kg) on opposite 30° frictionless inclines, connected over the top. The acceleration is:
Step 1 — Forces along string direction: $m_2$ pulls with $m_2 g\sin 30° = 6(10)(0.5) = 30$ N. $m_1$ pulls with $m_1 g\sin 30° = 2(10)(0.5) = 10$ N.
Step 2 — Net force: $30 - 10 = 20$ N (toward $m_2$'s side)
Step 3 — Total mass: $m_1 + m_2 = 8$ kg
Step 4: $a = 20/8 = 2.5$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: Double incline = two blocks pulling against each other via gravity components. The heavier block ($m_2 = 6$ kg) wins. Net force = difference in $mg\sin\theta$ values. Since both angles are 30°, it simplifies to $(m_2 - m_1)g\sin 30°$.
Q15Answer: (A) $6.4$ m/s$^2$
A 3 kg block on a rough table ($\mu_k = 0.2$) is connected over a pulley to a 7 kg hanging block. The acceleration is:
Step 1 — Friction on table block: $f_k = \mu_k m_1 g = 0.2 \times 3 \times 10 = 6$ N
Step 2 — Net driving force: $m_2 g - f_k = 70 - 6 = 64$ N
Step 3 — Total mass: $m_1 + m_2 = 10$ kg
Step 4: $a = 64/10 = 6.4$ m/s$^2$
Teacher's approach: This is a standard Atwood + friction. The hanging block provides the driving force ($m_2 g$), friction on the table block resists. Both blocks share the same acceleration and the same string tension. The system approach gives $a$ in one step.
Q16Answer: (A) $25.2$ N
In Q15, the tension in the string is:
Step 1 — Isolate the hanging block: $m_2 g - T = m_2 a$
Step 2 — Solve: $T = m_2(g - a) = 7(10 - 6.4) = 7(3.6) = 25.2$ N
Check: $T < m_2 g = 70$ N ✓ (hanging block accelerates downward, so $T$ must be less than its weight)
Teacher's approach: To find tension, isolate ONE block after finding $a$. Pick the hanging block: $T = m_2(g - a)$. Or verify with the table block: $T - f_k = m_1 a \Rightarrow T = m_1 a + f_k = 3(6.4) + 6 = 25.2$ ✓. Both approaches must give the same $T$.
Q17Answer: (A) $30$ N
A 3 kg hanging block and a 5 kg block on a frictionless 37° incline ($\sin 37° = 0.6$) are connected over a pulley. The system is in equilibrium. The tension is:
Step 1 — Equilibrium check: Hanging: $T = m_{hang}g = 3(10) = 30$ N. Incline: $T = m_{incline}g\sin 37° = 5(10)(0.6) = 30$ N. ✓ They match!
Step 2: Since the system is in equilibrium ($a = 0$), $T = 30$ N.
Teacher's approach: In equilibrium, the tension equals BOTH the hanging weight AND the incline gravity component. The fact that $3g = 5g\sin 37°$ (i.e., $30 = 30$) confirms equilibrium. If these weren't equal, the system wouldn't be in equilibrium.
Q18Answer: (A) $3g/5$
In an Atwood machine, if $m_2 = 4m_1$, the acceleration is:
Step 1 — Atwood formula: $a = \frac{m_2 - m_1}{m_1 + m_2}g$
Step 2 — Substitute $m_2 = 4m_1$: $a = \frac{4m_1 - m_1}{m_1 + 4m_1}g = \frac{3m_1}{5m_1}g = \frac{3g}{5}$
Teacher's approach: The Atwood formula $a = \frac{(m_2-m_1)}{(m_1+m_2)}g$ is worth memorizing. Notice: if $m_2 \gg m_1$, $a \to g$ (free fall). If $m_1 = m_2$, $a = 0$ (balanced). The formula always gives sensible limits.
Gotcha: (D) $3g/4$ = only divided by the heavier mass, not the total. (B) $g/2$ = used $m_2 = 2m_1$ instead of $4m_1$.
Category D: Circular Motion + Newton (Q19-23)
Q19Answer: (A) $\sqrt{10}$ m/s
A 2 kg ball on a 0.5 m string swings in a vertical circle. At the bottom, the tension is 60 N. The speed at the bottom is:
Step 1 — At the bottom: Center of circle is UP. Tension points toward center (up). Weight points away from center (down).
Step 2 — Newton toward center: $T - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
Step 3 — Solve: $60 - 2(10) = \frac{2v^2}{0.5} \Rightarrow 40 = 4v^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 10 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{10}$ m/s
Teacher's approach: For vertical circles, always ask: "Where is the center?" At the BOTTOM, center is UP. $T$ points UP (toward center, positive), $mg$ points DOWN (away, negative). So $T - mg = mv^2/r$. At the TOP: both point toward center, so $T + mg = mv^2/r$.
Gotcha: (B) $\sqrt{20}$ = forgot that $r = 0.5$, using $r = 1$ instead. Always check the radius!
Q20Answer: (A) $0.5$
A car drives at 20 m/s on a flat circular track of radius 80 m. The minimum $\mu_s$ to prevent sliding is:
Step 1 — Friction provides centripetal force: $\mu_s mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
Step 2 — Cancel $m$: $\mu_s = \frac{v^2}{rg} = \frac{400}{80 \times 10} = \frac{400}{800} = 0.5$
Teacher's approach: On a flat (unbanked) curve, friction is the only force providing centripetal acceleration. The formula $\mu_s = v^2/(rg)$ gives the minimum friction needed. If the road is wet and $\mu_s$ drops below this value, the car skids outward.
Q21Answer: (A) Decreases by factor $\sqrt{2}$
A satellite orbits Earth at radius $r$. If the orbit radius doubles to $2r$, the orbital speed:
Step 1 — Orbital speed formula: $\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$
Step 2 — At $2r$: $v' = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{2r}} = \frac{v}{\sqrt{2}}$
Step 3: Speed decreases by factor $\sqrt{2}$ (farther orbits are slower)
Teacher's approach: Key insight: farther orbits are SLOWER. This seems counterintuitive but makes physical sense — gravity is weaker at larger radii, so less centripetal force means less speed needed. $v \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$.
Q22Answer: (A) $5$ rad/s
A coin on a turntable at $r = 0.2$ m from center. If $\mu_s = 0.5$, the maximum angular velocity before it slides is:
Step 1 — Friction provides centripetal force: $\mu_s mg = m\omega^2 r$
Step 2 — Cancel $m$, solve for $\omega$: $\omega^2 = \frac{\mu_s g}{r} = \frac{0.5 \times 10}{0.2} = 25$
Step 3: $\omega = 5$ rad/s
Teacher's approach: For rotating platforms, use $\omega$ (not $v$) since it's uniform across the turntable. Note: coins farther from center slide first ($\omega_{max} \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$).
Gotcha: (B) $\sqrt{25} = 5$ — same answer, just written differently. (C) $\sqrt{50}$ would result from using $\mu_s = 1.0$ or $r = 0.1$.
Q23Answer: (A) $2mg$
A pendulum bob (mass $m$, length $L$) is released from 60° from vertical. The tension at the bottom is:
Step 1 — Energy conservation: Height drop: $h = L(1 - \cos 60°) = L(1 - 0.5) = L/2$
$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v^2 = 2g(L/2) = gL$
Step 2 — At the bottom (center is UP): $T - mg = \frac{mv^2}{L} = \frac{m(gL)}{L} = mg$
Step 3: $T = mg + mg = 2mg$
Teacher's approach: This combines energy AND Newton's Laws — the AP's favorite combination. Always use energy to find speed, THEN Newton for forces. General formula for pendulum released from angle $\theta_0$: $T_{bottom} = mg(3 - 2\cos\theta_0)$. At $60°$: $T = mg(3 - 1) = 2mg$ ✓. At $90°$ (horizontal): $T = 3mg$.
Gotcha: (B) $3mg$ = released from 90° (horizontal), not 60°. (C) $mg$ = forgot the centripetal term entirely.
Category E: Calculus-Based Newton (Q24-29)
Q24Answer: (A) $2$ m/s
A 4 kg object has force $F(t) = 12\sin(3t)$ N from $t = 0$ to $t = \pi/3$ s. Starting from rest, the final speed is:
Step 1 — Impulse: $J = \int_0^{\pi/3} 12\sin(3t)\,dt$
Step 2 — Integrate: $= 12\left[-\frac{\cos(3t)}{3}\right]_0^{\pi/3} = -4[\cos(\pi) - \cos(0)] = -4[-1 - 1] = 8$ N·s
Step 3 — Impulse-momentum: $J = mv \Rightarrow v = J/m = 8/4 = 2$ m/s
Teacher's approach: When force is a function of time, use the impulse-momentum theorem: $\int F\,dt = \Delta p$. The integral of $\sin$ gives $-\cos$. Watch the limits carefully — $\cos(\pi) = -1$ is where students make sign errors.
Gotcha: Don't forget the $1/3$ from the chain rule when integrating $\sin(3t)$. $\int\sin(3t)\,dt = -\cos(3t)/3$, not $-\cos(3t)$.
Q25Answer: (A) $5(1 - e^{-2t})$ m/s
A 5 kg object with drag $F = -10v$ falls under gravity. The velocity as a function of time is:
Step 1 — Newton's 2nd law: $m\frac{dv}{dt} = mg - bv \Rightarrow 5\frac{dv}{dt} = 50 - 10v$
Step 2 — Terminal velocity: Set $dv/dt = 0$: $v_t = mg/b = 50/10 = 5$ m/s
Step 3 — Separate variables: $\frac{dv}{50 - 10v} = \frac{dt}{5}$
Step 4 — Integrate: $-\frac{1}{10}\ln(50 - 10v) = \frac{t}{5} + C$. With $v(0) = 0$: $v(t) = 5(1 - e^{-2t})$
Teacher's approach: For linear drag problems, the answer is ALWAYS $v(t) = v_{terminal}(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ where $\tau = m/b$ is the time constant. Here $v_t = 5$ m/s and $\tau = 5/10 = 0.5$ s, so $1/\tau = 2$. This gives $v = 5(1 - e^{-2t})$ immediately.
Gotcha: (B) $5e^{-2t}$ = exponential DECAY, not growth. That would mean the object starts at 5 m/s and decelerates — opposite of falling from rest. (C) has the wrong time constant.
Q26Answer: (A) $10$ m/s
A 0.5 kg particle falls with quadratic drag $F_{drag} = -0.05v^2$ N. The terminal velocity is:
Step 1 — At terminal velocity, $a = 0$: $mg = 0.05v_t^2$
Step 2: $0.5 \times 10 = 0.05v_t^2 \Rightarrow 5 = 0.05v_t^2 \Rightarrow v_t^2 = 100$
Step 3: $v_t = 10$ m/s
Teacher's approach: Terminal velocity is always found by setting net force $= 0$. For quadratic drag: $v_t = \sqrt{mg/c}$. For linear drag: $v_t = mg/b$. Know both forms.
Q27Answer: (A) $2\pi/3$ s
A 2 kg mass on a spring with $k = 18$ N/m. The period of oscillation is:
Step 1 — Period formula: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$
Step 2: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{2/18} = 2\pi\sqrt{1/9} = 2\pi \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3}$ s
Teacher's approach: This is a direct formula application. $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{9} = 3$ rad/s. Then $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/3$ s. Know that heavier masses oscillate slower (larger $T$), stiffer springs oscillate faster (smaller $T$).
Q28Answer: (A) $24$ N
Water flows onto a stationary plate at rate 3 kg/s with horizontal velocity 8 m/s. The force on the plate is:
Step 1 — Newton's 2nd law (momentum form): $F = \frac{dp}{dt} = \frac{dm}{dt} \times v$
Step 2: $F = 3 \times 8 = 24$ N
Teacher's approach: When mass is flowing continuously, use $F = \dot{m}v$ where $\dot{m} = dm/dt$ is the mass flow rate. Each second, 3 kg of water arrives with velocity 8 m/s and is stopped (momentum goes to zero). The plate must absorb that momentum: $\Delta p/\Delta t = 3 \times 8 = 24$ N.
Q29Answer: (A) $10$ N
A 5 kg object has $a(t) = 6 - 2t$ m/s$^2$. The net force at $t = 2$ s is:
Step 1: $a(2) = 6 - 2(2) = 6 - 4 = 2$ m/s$^2$
Step 2: $F = ma = 5 \times 2 = 10$ N
Teacher's approach: Even though $a(t)$ is time-dependent, $F = ma$ still applies at each instant. Just plug in the specific time, get $a$, multiply by $m$. This is Newton's 2nd law — it's always instantaneous.
Category F: Advanced / Conceptual (Q30-35)
Q30Answer: (A) $500$ N
A tug-of-war team pulls with 500 N on a rope. The other team also pulls with 500 N. The tension in the rope is:
Key concept: Tension in a rope is the force transmitted through it, which equals the force applied by EITHER end.
Analogy: Hang a 500 N weight from a rope attached to a ceiling. The ceiling pulls up 500 N, the weight pulls down 500 N. Tension = 500 N, not 1000 N.
Result: $T = 500$ N
Teacher's approach: This is the classic Newton's 3rd law conceptual question. Think of it this way: cut the rope at any point and ask "what force holds the two halves together?" It's 500 N. The rope doesn't "add" forces from both sides. Each side pulls with 500 N, and the rope transmits 500 N.
Gotcha: (B) 1000 N is the most common wrong answer. Students think "500 from each side = 1000 total." But that's not how tension works. If that logic were correct, a 500 N weight hanging from a ceiling would create 1000 N of tension — absurd!
Q31Answer: (A) $\sqrt{gR}$
A car goes over a hill of radius $R$ at speed $v$. The driver feels weightless. The speed must be:
Step 1 — Weightless: Normal force $= 0$ (the seat doesn't push up on the driver)
Step 2 — At the top of the hill (center is DOWN): $mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{R}$
Step 3 — Set $N = 0$: $mg = \frac{mv^2}{R} \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{gR}$
Teacher's approach: "Weightless" in physics means normal force $= 0$. It doesn't mean gravity is gone — gravity is still providing the centripetal force! This is the same condition as orbital motion. At the hill top, gravity alone provides all the centripetal force needed.
Q32Answer: (A) $\frac{40}{\cos 45° + 0.4\sin 45°}$ N
A box on a rough flat surface ($\mu_s = 0.4$) has a rope pulling at 45° above horizontal. The tension needed to START moving the box (mass 10 kg) is:
Step 1 — Vertical equilibrium: $N + T\sin 45° = mg \Rightarrow N = mg - T\sin 45°$
Step 2 — Horizontal (on verge): $T\cos 45° = \mu_s N = \mu_s(mg - T\sin 45°)$
Step 3 — Solve for $T$: $T\cos 45° + \mu_s T\sin 45° = \mu_s mg$
$T(\cos 45° + 0.4\sin 45°) = 0.4 \times 100 = 40$
$T = \frac{40}{\cos 45° + 0.4\sin 45°}$
Teacher's approach: When a force pulls at an angle above horizontal, it does TWO things: (1) pulls the object forward (horizontal component), (2) lifts the object slightly (reducing the normal force). A reduced normal force means less friction. This is why pulling at an angle requires LESS force than pulling horizontally! The optimal angle to minimize $T$ is $\theta = \arctan(\mu_s)$.
Gotcha: (B) 40 N = assumed $T = \mu_s mg$ without accounting for the reduced normal force. (C) $40/\cos 45°$ = forgot that the upward component of $T$ reduces $N$.
Q33Answer: (A) $3.6$ kg
Block A ($6$ kg) is on a 37° frictionless incline, connected to hanging Block B. Equilibrium requires $m_B =$:
Step 1 — Equilibrium: Weight of B = incline component of A: $m_B g = m_A g\sin 37°$
Step 2: $m_B = m_A \sin 37° = 6 \times 0.6 = 3.6$ kg
Teacher's approach: In equilibrium, the hanging block's weight must exactly balance the gravity component pulling Block A down the incline. Since the incline "dilutes" gravity by $\sin\theta$, the hanging mass is always less than the incline mass (for $\theta < 90°$).
Q34Answer: (A) $Ft$ (force × time)
Which expression has the SAME units as momentum (kg·m/s)?
Step 1 — Check each:
$(A)$ $Ft$: $[\text{N} \cdot \text{s}] = [\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2 \cdot \text{s}] = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$ ✓ — this is impulse!
$(B)$ $Fv$: $[\text{N} \cdot \text{m/s}] = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}^3 = $ watts ✗
$(C)$ $mv^2$: $[\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2] = $ joules ✗
$(D)$ $ma$: $[\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2] = $ newtons ✗
$(E)$ $Fd$: $[\text{N}\cdot\text{m}] = $ joules ✗
Teacher's approach: This is the impulse-momentum theorem: $J = F\Delta t = \Delta p$. Impulse has the same units as momentum. This is one of the most important dimensional analysis results to memorize. Also: $Fv = $ power, $Fd = $ work, $ma = $ force.
Q35Answer: (A) $25\sqrt{3}$ N
A 5 kg lamp hangs from two ropes: one at 30° from vertical and one at 60° from vertical. The tension in the rope at 30° from vertical is:
Step 1 — Horizontal equilibrium: $T_1\sin 30° = T_2\sin 60°$
$T_2 = T_1 \cdot \frac{\sin 30°}{\sin 60°} = T_1 \cdot \frac{0.5}{\sqrt{3}/2} = \frac{T_1}{\sqrt{3}}$
Step 2 — Vertical equilibrium: $T_1\cos 30° + T_2\cos 60° = mg = 50$
$T_1 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{T_1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 50$
Step 3 — Simplify: $T_1\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}\right) = T_1\left(\frac{3}{2\sqrt{3}} + \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}}\right) = T_1 \cdot \frac{4}{2\sqrt{3}} = \frac{2T_1}{\sqrt{3}} = 50$
Step 4: $T_1 = \frac{50\sqrt{3}}{2} = 25\sqrt{3} \approx 43.3$ N
Teacher's approach: For two-rope problems: (1) Set up horizontal and vertical equilibrium. (2) Use the horizontal equation to express $T_2$ in terms of $T_1$. (3) Substitute into the vertical equation and solve. The rope closer to vertical (30° from vertical) carries MORE weight — this makes physical sense since it's more "upright."
Gotcha: (B) 25 N = assumed each rope carries half the weight. Wrong! The ropes are at different angles, so they carry different loads. (D) $50/\sqrt{3}$ = computed $T_2$ (the other rope) instead of $T_1$.

FRQ 1: Block-Spring-Incline System15 points
A block of mass $m = 2.0$ kg is placed against a spring (spring constant $k = 500$ N/m) that is compressed by $d = 0.30$ m on a frictionless horizontal surface. The spring is released, launching the block toward a rough incline that makes an angle $\theta = 30°$ with the horizontal. The coefficient of kinetic friction on the incline is $\mu_k = 0.20$.
(a) Speed leaving the spring (3 pts)
Determine the speed of the block just as it leaves the spring.
Energy conservation (horizontal, frictionless): $\frac{1}{2}kd^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
Solve: $v = d\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = 0.30\sqrt{\frac{500}{2}} = 0.30\sqrt{250} = 0.30 \times 5\sqrt{10}$
Result: $v \approx 4.74$ m/s
Scoring: 1 pt energy equation, 1 pt correct substitution, 1 pt answer with units
(b) Distance up the incline (4 pts)
Determine the distance the block travels up the incline before coming momentarily to rest.
Energy conservation with friction: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgs\sin\theta + \mu_k mg\cos\theta \cdot s$
Factor: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgs(\sin\theta + \mu_k\cos\theta)$
Solve: $s = \frac{v^2}{2g(\sin\theta + \mu_k\cos\theta)} = \frac{22.5}{2(10)(0.5 + 0.2 \times 0.866)}$
Compute: $= \frac{22.5}{20(0.5 + 0.1732)} = \frac{22.5}{13.46} \approx 1.67$ m
Scoring: 1 pt energy equation with friction, 1 pt correct friction force, 1 pt algebra, 1 pt answer
(c) Does the block slide back down? (3 pts)
Does the block slide back down the incline? Justify your answer with a calculation.
At rest on incline, compare forces along incline:
Gravity component (down): $mg\sin 30° = 2(10)(0.5) = 10$ N
Max static friction (up, opposing slide down): Using $\mu_k$ as lower bound: $\mu_k mg\cos 30° = 0.2(2)(10)(0.866) = 3.46$ N
Since $10 > 3.46$: Gravity component exceeds friction. YES, the block slides back down.
Equivalently: $\tan 30° = 0.577 > \mu_k = 0.2$, so the incline is too steep for friction to hold the block.
Scoring: 1 pt identifying forces, 1 pt correct comparison, 1 pt conclusion with justification
(d) Normal force at top of frictionless quarter-circle (5 pts)
If instead the incline were frictionless but curved (quarter-circle of radius $R$), derive an expression for the normal force on the block at the top of the curve as a function of $m$, $k$, $d$, $R$, and $g$.
Energy conservation to height $R$: $\frac{1}{2}kd^2 = mgR + \frac{1}{2}mv_{top}^2$
Solve for $v_{top}^2$: $v_{top}^2 = \frac{kd^2}{m} - 2gR$
At the top of the quarter-circle (centripetal direction = downward toward center): $mg - N = \frac{mv_{top}^2}{R}$
Solve for $N$: $N = mg - \frac{mv_{top}^2}{R} = mg - \frac{m}{R}\left(\frac{kd^2}{m} - 2gR\right)$
Simplify: $$N = mg - \frac{kd^2}{R} + 2mg = 3mg - \frac{kd^2}{R}$$
Scoring: 1 pt energy equation, 1 pt solving for $v^2$, 1 pt centripetal equation, 1 pt substitution, 1 pt clean final expression
Note: If $kd^2/R > 3mg$, then $N < 0$, meaning the block would leave the track. The block stays on if $R \geq kd^2/(3mg)$.
FRQ 2: Rotating Rod with Attached Mass15 points
A thin uniform rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ is free to rotate about a frictionless pivot at its left end. A small ball of mass $M$ is attached to the right end of the rod. The system is held horizontal and then released from rest.
(a) Moment of inertia about the pivot (2 pts)
Calculate the moment of inertia of the system about the pivot point.
Rod about one end: $I_{rod} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2$
Ball (point mass at distance $L$): $I_{ball} = ML^2$
Total: $I = \frac{1}{3}ML^2 + ML^2 = \frac{4}{3}ML^2$
Scoring: 1 pt each term, or 2 pts for correct total
(b) Angular acceleration immediately after release (4 pts)
Determine the angular acceleration of the system immediately after release.
Torque about pivot (horizontal position): $\tau = Mg\frac{L}{2} + MgL = \frac{3MgL}{2}$
Newton's 2nd law for rotation: $\tau = I\alpha$
Solve: $\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{3MgL/2}{4ML^2/3} = \frac{3MgL}{2} \times \frac{3}{4ML^2} = \frac{9g}{8L}$
Scoring: 1 pt torque from rod, 1 pt torque from ball, 1 pt $\tau = I\alpha$, 1 pt answer
(c) Angular velocity when vertical (4 pts)
Determine the angular velocity of the system when the rod passes through the vertical position.
Energy conservation: Both centers of mass drop by their distance from pivot.
Height drop: Rod CM drops $L/2$. Ball drops $L$.
Energy equation: $Mg\frac{L}{2} + MgL = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
$\frac{3MgL}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{4ML^2}{3} \cdot \omega^2 = \frac{2ML^2}{3}\omega^2$
Solve: $\omega^2 = \frac{3 \times 3MgL}{2 \times 2ML^2} = \frac{9g}{4L}$
$\omega = \frac{3}{2}\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}$
Scoring: 1 pt energy conservation setup, 1 pt correct height drops, 1 pt using $I$ from part (a), 1 pt answer
(d) Pivot force when vertical (5 pts)
At the instant the rod is vertical, determine the magnitude and direction of the force exerted by the pivot on the rod-ball system.
Step 1 — Angular acceleration when vertical: When vertical, all weight acts along the rod (through the pivot line). Net torque $= 0$, so $\alpha = 0$. Only centripetal acceleration matters.
Step 2 — Center of mass location: $r_{cm} = \frac{M(L/2) + ML}{2M} = \frac{3L}{4}$
Step 3 — Centripetal acceleration of CM (upward, toward pivot): $a_{cm} = \omega^2 r_{cm} = \frac{9g}{4L} \cdot \frac{3L}{4} = \frac{27g}{16}$
Step 4 — Newton's 2nd law (upward positive): $F_{pivot} - 2Mg = 2M \cdot a_{cm}$
$F_{pivot} = 2Mg + 2M \cdot \frac{27g}{16} = 2Mg + \frac{27Mg}{8}$
$F_{pivot} = \frac{16Mg + 27Mg}{8} = \frac{43Mg}{8}$
Direction: Upward (toward the pivot, which is at the top when the rod hangs vertically)
Scoring: 1 pt $\alpha = 0$ at vertical, 1 pt CM location, 1 pt centripetal acceleration, 1 pt Newton's 2nd law, 1 pt magnitude + direction
FRQ 3: Oscillating System & Experimental Design15 points
A block of mass $m$ is attached to two identical springs, each with spring constant $k$, arranged in parallel (both connected between the block and a wall). The block slides on a frictionless surface.
(a) Effective spring constant and period (3 pts)
Show that the effective spring constant of the parallel combination is $k_{eff} = 2k$. Derive the period of oscillation.
Parallel springs: When displaced by $x$, EACH spring exerts force $kx$ in the restoring direction.
Total restoring force: $F = kx + kx = 2kx$. Therefore $k_{eff} = 2k$.
Period: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k_{eff}}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{2k}}$
Scoring: 1 pt showing each spring exerts $kx$, 1 pt $k_{eff} = 2k$, 1 pt period formula
(b) Velocity as function of position (3 pts)
If the block is displaced by $A$ from equilibrium and released, derive an expression for the velocity as a function of position $x$.
Energy conservation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 = \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}x^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
Solve for $v$: $v^2 = \frac{k_{eff}}{m}(A^2 - x^2) = \frac{2k}{m}(A^2 - x^2)$
Result: $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2k}{m}}\sqrt{A^2 - x^2}$$
Scoring: 1 pt energy equation, 1 pt algebra, 1 pt final expression
(c) Amplitude after one cycle with friction (4 pts)
Now suppose the surface has friction coefficient $\mu_k$ (kinetic) and the block is released from amplitude $A$. Determine the amplitude after one complete oscillation (one full cycle). State any approximations.
Approximation: Assume friction is small and amplitude changes slowly (so the path is approximately sinusoidal).
Distance traveled in one full cycle: $\approx 4A$ (right to equilibrium, to $-A$, back to equilibrium, back to $+A'$)
Energy lost to friction: $\Delta E = \mu_k mg \times 4A$
Energy equation: $\frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A^2 - \frac{1}{2}k_{eff}A'^2 = \mu_k mg \cdot 4A$
$k(A^2 - A'^2) = 4\mu_k mgA$
$k(A + A')(A - A') = 4\mu_k mgA$
With small friction ($A' \approx A$): $A + A' \approx 2A$, so:
$2kA(A - A') \approx 4\mu_k mgA$
$A - A' = \frac{2\mu_k mg}{k}$
Result: $$A' = A - \frac{2\mu_k mg}{k}$$
Scoring: 1 pt distance = 4A, 1 pt energy lost to friction, 1 pt energy balance equation, 1 pt result with stated approximation
(d) Experimental design (5 pts)
Design an experiment to determine the spring constant $k$ using only a ruler, a set of known masses, and a stopwatch. Describe your procedure, state what you would measure, and explain how you would analyze the data.
Procedure:
1. Attach both springs in parallel to a wall and connect them to a block on a smooth surface.
2. Place a known mass $m$ on the block.
3. Displace the block a small distance from equilibrium and release.
4. Use the stopwatch to time $N$ complete oscillations (e.g., $N = 10$).
5. Compute the period: $T = \text{total time}/N$.
6. Repeat for 5+ different masses.
Measurements: Mass $m$ (known), total time for $N$ oscillations (stopwatch), number of oscillations $N$.
Analysis: From $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/(2k)}$, squaring gives: $T^2 = \frac{2\pi^2}{k}m$
Graph: Plot $T^2$ (y-axis) vs. $m$ (x-axis). The result is a straight line through the origin with slope $= 2\pi^2/k$.
Extract $k$: $k = \frac{2\pi^2}{\text{slope}}$
Scoring: 1 pt clear procedure, 1 pt identifying measurements, 1 pt linearized equation, 1 pt correct graph (axes labeled), 1 pt extracting $k$ from slope
Alternative method (static): Hang the spring vertically. Add known masses $m$ one at a time. Measure the extension $x$ with the ruler. Plot $F = mg$ vs $x$. Slope $= k_{eff} = 2k$, so $k = \text{slope}/2$. This is simpler but doesn't use the stopwatch.
