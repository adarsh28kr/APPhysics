WORK & ENERGY REFRESHER

AP Physics C: Mechanics • 12 Topic Areas • Formulas, Intuition & AP Tips
The Big Idea
$W_{net} = \Delta KE$   •   $KE_i + U_i + W_{nc} = KE_f + U_f$
Energy can't be created or destroyed — only transferred between forms. Work is the mechanism of transfer.

1 Work by a Constant Force

$W = Fd\cos\theta$
$\theta$ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.
$\theta = 0°$
$W = Fd$
Force along motion
$\theta = 90°$
$W = 0$
Force perpendicular
$\theta = 180°$
$W = -Fd$
Force opposes motion
Normal force and gravity do zero work when an object moves horizontally (both are perpendicular to displacement, $\theta = 90°$).
$\theta$ is NOT the incline angle. It's the angle between $\vec{F}$ and $\vec{d}$. Draw both vectors and measure between them.

2 Work by a Variable Force

$W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x)\,dx$
Graphically: Work = area under the $F$-$x$ graph. Positive area above the axis, negative below.
Spring example: $W_{spring} = \int_0^x (-kx)\,dx = -\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ (spring does negative work when stretched).
AP asks: "$F(x) = 6x^2$ N. Find the work from $x = 1$ to $x = 3$ m."

3 Work-Energy Theorem

The NET work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.
$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$
This is the most powerful equation in mechanics. It works even when forces are variable, paths are curved, or acceleration isn't constant. Net work = individual works added up.
AP asks: "100 J of net work is done on a 2 kg object at rest. Find its final speed."

4 Kinetic Energy

$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
Always positive. Depends on speed squared — double the speed = 4× the KE. Triple = 9×.
Stopping distance: A car at $2v$ needs $4\times$ the stopping distance (because it has $4\times$ the KE to dissipate).

5 Gravitational Potential Energy

Near surface:
$U_g = mgh$
$h$ measured from chosen reference
General (planets/orbits):
$U_g = -\dfrac{GMm}{r}$
Always negative, zero at $r = \infty$
$h$ is relative. You choose where $h = 0$. Pick the most convenient reference point (usually the lowest point in the problem).
Why the minus sign? In the general formula, objects bound by gravity have negative total energy. You must add energy to escape (reach $U = 0$ at infinity).

6 Spring / Elastic PE

$U_s = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$
$x$ is measured from the natural (unstretched) length. Compressed or stretched both give positive PE.
Spring launch: $\frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ → $v = x\sqrt{k/m}$. All spring PE converts to KE when the object leaves the spring.
Don't forget to square $x$! Compressing twice as far stores 4× the energy.

7 Conservation of Energy

$KE_i + U_i + W_{nc} = KE_f + U_f$
If no friction/air resistance ($W_{nc} = 0$): total mechanical energy is conserved.
$\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mgh_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mgh_f$
When to use energy vs. forces: "Find speed at some height" → energy. "Find acceleration or force" → $F = ma$. "Find time" → kinematics.
AP asks: "A ball is released from height $h$ and slides down a frictionless track. Find its speed at the bottom."

8 Non-Conservative Work (Friction)

$W_{friction} = -f_k \cdot d = -\mu_k mg \cdot d$
Friction always does negative work (opposes motion). It removes mechanical energy from the system, converting it to heat.
$\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mgh_i - f_k d = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mgh_f$
Energy budget approach: Start with initial energy, subtract friction losses, the remainder becomes final KE + PE. Track each term step by step.
AP asks: "Block slides down rough incline, then along rough floor, then up a frictionless ramp. Find max height."

9 Power

Average: $P_{avg} = \dfrac{W}{t} = \dfrac{\Delta E}{t}$
Instantaneous: $P = \dfrac{dW}{dt} = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v} = Fv\cos\theta$
Units: Watts (W) = Joules/second.   1 horsepower = 746 W.
Constant velocity up a hill: Engine force balances gravity + friction. $P = F_{engine} \cdot v$. This is the most common AP power problem.
AP asks: "A car drives at constant $v$ up a hill. Find the power output."

10 Potential Energy Curves

$F(x) = -\dfrac{dU}{dx}$
Given $U(x)$, find force: Take the negative derivative. Force points from high $U$ to low $U$ (downhill on the PE curve).
Reading a $U(x)$ graph:
• Equilibrium: where slope $= 0$ ($F = 0$)
• Turning points: where $U(x) = E_{total}$ (KE = 0, particle reverses)
• Allowed region: where $U(x) \le E_{total}$ (KE can't be negative)
• Max KE: where $U$ is minimum (all energy is kinetic)
AP asks: "Given $U(x)$ graph with total energy $E$, identify turning points and the region where the particle is trapped."

11 Stability Analysis

$\dfrac{d^2U}{dx^2} > 0$ → Stable
Local minimum (valley)
$\dfrac{d^2U}{dx^2} < 0$ → Unstable
Local maximum (hilltop)
Physical intuition: A ball in a valley (stable) returns when nudged. A ball on a hilltop (unstable) rolls away when nudged. A ball on a flat surface (neutral) stays wherever you put it.
Don't confuse min/max of $U$ with stable/unstable! Minimum of $U$ = stable. Maximum of $U$ = unstable. (Think: ball rolls to lowest energy.)

12 Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces

Conservative
Gravity, spring, electrostatic
Work is path-independent
Has a PE function
$W_{loop} = 0$
Non-Conservative
Friction, air resistance, applied force
Work depends on path
No PE function
$W_{loop} \neq 0$
Test for conservative: Does the work depend on the path taken? If the work from A to B is the same regardless of the route, it's conservative. Friction fails this — longer path = more work.

When to Use Energy Methods

  • "Find speed at some position" → Energy conservation
  • "Find height / distance" → Energy conservation
  • "How far does it slide before stopping?" → Energy with friction
  • "Find force at a position" → $F = -dU/dx$ or Newton's laws
  • "Find time" → NOT energy — use kinematics
  • "Find acceleration" → NOT energy — use $F = ma$
  • "Find power" → $P = Fv$ or $P = dW/dt$
